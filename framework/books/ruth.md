## Purpose

This module helps you approach Ruth as a tightly crafted short narrative — set in
the time of the judges — about loyalty, kinship, and provision worked out through
ordinary people. It is a hermeneutic profile, not a commentary: it tells you what
to notice and ask, assuming Core, Genre, and Context are already loaded. It does
not tell you what Ruth means.

## When to apply

When interpreting any passage in Ruth, on top of [[genre.narrative]], attending
especially to the honor-shame and kinship world in [[context.honor-shame]].

## Genre signals

A short, unified **narrative** ([[genre.narrative]]) — a carefully plotted novella
with strong dialogue and a clear arc from emptiness to fullness. It closes with a
**genealogy** that reframes the whole. Read the dialogue and scene-craft closely;
little is incidental.

## Historical anchors

The **honor-shame** and kinship structures of ancient Israel
([[context.honor-shame]]) — family loyalty (*ḥesed*), the kinsman-redeemer
(*go'el*), levirate-type marriage, gleaning rights, and the vulnerability of
widows and foreigners — set within the wider **ancient Near East**
([[context.ancient-near-east]]). The book's date and setting are debated; hold
them with appropriate confidence (see [[core.epistemic-humility]]).

## Literary features

Look for **structural symmetry** (emptiness to fullness), **key repeated words**
(*ḥesed*, redeem, return, wings/refuge), **dialogue** that carries the action,
**legal and customary scenes** (the threshing floor, the transaction at the gate),
**irony** and **wordplay** in names, and the closing **genealogy** that links Ruth
to David (see [[core.intertextuality]]). Do not assume every feature is present in
a given passage; look for them.

## Key interpretive questions

Pose, do not answer:

- How does this scene advance the movement from loss toward restoration?
- What kinship custom, legal practice, or social obligation is in play, and what would the ancient audience have assumed?
- How is loyalty (*ḥesed*) being shown, and by whom?
- Is the passage reporting custom or commending a character's conduct?
- How does Ruth's status as a Moabite foreigner shape the episode?
- What does the closing genealogy do to the way the story is read?

## Common misreadings

- Reducing the book to a romance and missing its themes of loyalty, provision, and inclusion.
- Misreading the kinship-redeemer and threshing-floor scenes through modern customs rather than ancient ones.
- Flattening the genealogy into an appendix rather than a key to the book's purpose.
- Allegorizing characters into ciphers instead of reading the story's own movement.

## Handling uncertainty

The book's date, purpose, and the precise legal background of redemption and
levirate practice are debated. Present the range, distinguish observation from
reconstruction, and label confidence (see [[core.epistemic-humility]]); leave
conclusions to the reader.
