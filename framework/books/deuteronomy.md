## Purpose

This module helps you approach Deuteronomy as Moses' covenant address on the edge
of the land — law restated and preached, framed as a binding relationship between
the LORD and Israel. It is a hermeneutic profile, not a commentary: it tells you
what to notice and ask, assuming Core, Genre, and Context are already loaded. It
does not tell you what Deuteronomy means.

## When to apply

When interpreting any passage in Deuteronomy, on top of [[genre.law]] — the book
is sustained covenant instruction — and the **narrative** frame
([[genre.narrative]]) of Moses' farewell speeches.

## Genre signals

Predominantly **law** ([[genre.law]]) in a distinctive **preached, hortatory**
mode — exposition and exhortation, not bare statute — cast as **speeches** within
a thin **narrative** frame ([[genre.narrative]]). It is widely read against the
form of an ancient **treaty/covenant** document. Embedded **poetry** appears
(e.g., the Song and the blessing of Moses).

## Historical anchors

The **covenant** ([[context.covenant]]) is central: many note the book's
resemblance to **ancient Near Eastern treaty** structures
([[context.ancient-near-east]]) — historical prologue, stipulations, blessings and
curses, witnesses. The composition, date, and setting of Deuteronomy are debated
and tradition-linked; hold them with appropriate confidence
(see [[core.epistemic-humility]]).

## Literary features

Look for the **treaty/covenant structure**, **sermonic repetition** and motive
clauses ("so that it may go well with you"), the **Shema** and centralizing
emphases, **blessing and curse** sections, embedded **poetry**, and Deuteronomy's
heavy **reuse of earlier Torah** as well as its dense influence on later Scripture
(see [[core.intertextuality]]). Do not assume every feature governs a given
passage; look for them.

## Key interpretive questions

Pose, do not answer:

- How does this passage function within the covenant address — as motivation, stipulation, blessing/curse, or narrative frame?
- How does it restate, expand, or recast earlier Torah, and why might it do so here?
- What does the treaty/covenant frame contribute to reading this passage?
- What would the original audience, poised to enter the land, have heard that a modern reader might miss?
- How does the preached, hortatory style shape the law's claim on the hearer?
- How is this material taken up later in Scripture?

## Common misreadings

- Reading Deuteronomy as flat legislation while missing its preached, persuasive character.
- Ignoring the treaty/covenant frame that shapes the whole.
- Treating its restatement of earlier law as mere repetition rather than asking why it recasts.
- Settling the contested question of a command's ongoing application by assumption rather than careful, stated reasoning.

## Handling uncertainty

The book's composition and date, its relation to ANE treaties, and how (or
whether) its laws apply to later readers are debated and tradition-linked. Present
the major approaches, separate observation from reconstruction, and label
confidence (see [[core.epistemic-humility]]); leave the conclusion to the reader.
