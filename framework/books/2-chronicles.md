## Purpose

This module helps you approach 2 Chronicles as the continuation of the
Chronicler's retelling — Solomon and the temple, then the kings of Judah down to
exile and the hope of return, read for the post-exilic community. It is a
hermeneutic profile, not a commentary: it tells you what to notice and ask,
assuming Core, Genre, and Context are already loaded. It does not tell you what
2 Chronicles means.

## When to apply

When interpreting any passage in 2 Chronicles, on top of [[genre.narrative]], the
post-exilic perspective ([[context.exile-and-restoration]]), and the monarchy it
retells ([[context.israelite-monarchy]]).

## Genre signals

Predominantly **narrative** ([[genre.narrative]]) — the temple under Solomon and
the reigns of Judah's kings — with embedded **prayers, speeches, and sermons** that
voice the book's concerns. Read it as shaped, selective retelling rather than
neutral chronicle.

## Historical anchors

Written for the **post-exilic / restoration** community ([[context.exile-and-restoration]]),
retelling Judah's **monarchy** ([[context.israelite-monarchy]]) with the **temple**
and its worship at the center ([[context.temple]]). The narrative focuses on
Judah (largely omitting the northern kingdom) and ends turned toward return. Date
and sources are debated; hold them with appropriate confidence
(see [[core.epistemic-humility]]).

## Literary features

Look for the **temple-centered** structure, the focus on **Judah's kings** and
their reforms or failures, recurring patterns of **seeking the LORD, repentance,
and consequence**, **speeches and prayers** that interpret events, the selective
reshaping of the **Kings** material (see [[core.intertextuality]]), and the closing
note of hope for return. Do not assume every feature is present in a given
passage; look for them.

## Key interpretive questions

Pose, do not answer:

- How does this passage evaluate the king or the people, and against what standard?
- How does the Chronicler's version differ from the parallel in Kings, and what might it emphasize?
- Is the passage reporting events or commending a response?
- Why is the temple and its worship so central here?
- What would the post-exilic audience have heard that a modern reader might miss?
- How does this passage reuse and reshape earlier Scripture?

## Common misreadings

- Reading Chronicles as a duplicate of Kings and missing its distinct, temple-centered emphases.
- Treating the differences from Kings as mere error rather than purposeful selection — a question to weigh, not assume.
- Reading the patterns of reward and consequence as a mechanical formula rather than as the book's framing.
- Treating narrated royal actions as automatically endorsed or prescribed.

## Handling uncertainty

The date, sources, and aims of Chronicles, and the reasons behind specific
differences from Kings, are debated. Present the range, distinguish observation
from reconstruction, and label confidence (see [[core.epistemic-humility]]); leave
conclusions to the reader.
