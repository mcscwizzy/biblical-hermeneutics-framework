## Purpose

This module helps you approach 2 Samuel as the narrative of David's reign — his
rise, the covenant promise to his house, and the consequences that follow his
failures. It is a hermeneutic profile, not a commentary: it tells you what to
notice and ask, assuming Core, Genre, and Context are already loaded. It does not
tell you what 2 Samuel means.

## When to apply

When interpreting any passage in 2 Samuel, on top of [[genre.narrative]] and the
world of the monarchy in [[context.israelite-monarchy]].

## Genre signals

Predominantly **narrative** ([[genre.narrative]]) — a sustained, psychologically
rich court history — framed by embedded **poetry** (e.g., David's laments and the
closing songs) and **prophetic** speech (Nathan). Read it as shaped, evaluative
storytelling attentive to point of view.

## Historical anchors

The **monarchy** under David ([[context.israelite-monarchy]]) — kingship,
dynasty, the court, and Jerusalem — and especially the **covenant** promise to
David's house ([[context.covenant]]), against the wider **ancient Near East**
([[context.ancient-near-east]]) of royal ideology and warfare. The history is
debated; hold reconstructions with appropriate confidence
(see [[core.epistemic-humility]]).

## Literary features

Look for the **Davidic covenant** and its echoes, **prophetic confrontation** and
its narrative consequences, **chiastic and paneled structure**, embedded **poetry**
that frames the book, **type-scenes**, recurring motifs (the "house" wordplay,
sin and consequence), and the heavy later **reuse** of the promise to David
across Scripture (see [[core.intertextuality]]). Do not assume every feature is
present in a given passage; look for them.

## Key interpretive questions

Pose, do not answer:

- How does this episode develop David's reign and the promise to his house?
- How does the narrator evaluate David's actions — and how does the account hold promise and failure together?
- Is the passage reporting events or commending a response?
- What does the covenant with David contribute to reading this passage?
- What would the ancient audience have assumed about kingship and dynasty that a modern reader might miss?
- How is the promise to David taken up later in Scripture?

## Common misreadings

- Idealizing David and minimizing the narrative's frank treatment of his failures, or the reverse.
- Treating narrated royal actions as automatically endorsed or prescribed.
- Reading the dynastic promise apart from how the narrative itself frames it.
- Assigning characters motives the text does not state.

## Handling uncertainty

The history of David's reign, the composition of Samuel, and the scope of the
dynastic promise are debated and, in places, tradition-linked. Present the range,
distinguish observation from reconstruction, and label confidence
(see [[core.epistemic-humility]]); leave conclusions to the reader.
