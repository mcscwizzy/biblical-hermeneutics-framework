## Purpose

This module helps you approach 2 Peter as a farewell-style letter warning against
false teachers and scoffers, and reaffirming the certainty of the Lord's coming and
the reliability of the prophetic word. It is a hermeneutic profile, not a commentary:
it tells you what to notice and ask, assuming Core, Genre, and Context are already
loaded. It does not tell you what 2 Peter means.

## When to apply

When interpreting any passage in 2 Peter, on top of [[genre.epistle]] and the
Greco-Roman and Second Temple thought world it engages
([[context.greco-roman-world]], [[context.second-temple-judaism]]).

## Genre signals

An **epistle** ([[genre.epistle]]) cast in a **testamentary** mode (a farewell
charge anticipating the author's death), with strong **polemic** against false
teachers and an **apologetic** defense of the Lord's coming. Note that its middle
section closely **parallels Jude**. Read the testament, polemic, and defense
according to their aims.

## Historical anchors

The **Greco-Roman** intellectual setting ([[context.greco-roman-world]]) — including
skepticism about divine judgment — and the **Second Temple Jewish**
([[context.second-temple-judaism]]) traditions the letter draws on. Authorship and
date are debated more than for most New Testament books, partly on style and the
relationship to Jude; present the major approaches and weigh the evidence
(see [[core.epistemic-humility]]).

## Literary features

Look for the **testament** framing, the **denunciation of false teachers** (closely
paralleling Jude — see [[core.intertextuality]]), the **scoffers** and the **delay**
of the Lord's coming, the appeal to **eyewitness** and **prophetic** reliability, the
striking comment on **Paul's letters**, and reuse of **Old Testament** judgment
examples; **Greek** wording can bear on meaning (see [[language.greek]]). Do not
assume every feature is present in a given passage; look for them.

## Key interpretive questions

Pose, do not answer:

- What false teaching or doubt is this passage countering?
- How does the testamentary framing shape the exhortation?
- How does the passage relate to the parallel material in Jude, and what approaches exist to that relationship?
- How does the letter defend the certainty of the Lord's coming against the "scoffers"?
- What major approaches exist to the letter's authorship, and what is the evidence?

## Common misreadings

- Settling the debated authorship by assumption rather than weighing the evidence.
- Reading the false-teacher polemic apart from its rhetorical, testamentary purpose.
- Treating the parallels with Jude without asking about their literary relationship.
- Building a detached end-times scheme from the "day of the Lord" material.

## Handling uncertainty

Authorship, date, and the direction of the literary relationship with Jude are
debated and read differently across traditions. Present the major responsible
interpretations and their basis, label confidence (see [[core.epistemic-humility]]),
and leave the conclusion to the reader.
