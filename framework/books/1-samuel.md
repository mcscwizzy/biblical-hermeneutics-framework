## Purpose

This module helps you approach 1 Samuel as the narrative of Israel's transition to
monarchy — Samuel, Saul, and the rise of David — and the tensions that
accompany kingship. It is a hermeneutic profile, not a commentary: it tells you
what to notice and ask, assuming Core, Genre, and Context are already loaded. It
does not tell you what 1 Samuel means.

## When to apply

When interpreting any passage in 1 Samuel, on top of [[genre.narrative]] and the
world of emerging kingship in [[context.israelite-monarchy]].

## Genre signals

Predominantly **narrative** ([[genre.narrative]]) — closely observed character and
political drama — framed by embedded **poetry** (e.g., Hannah's song) and
prophetic **speech**. Read it as shaped, evaluative storytelling, attentive to
point of view.

## Historical anchors

The **rise of the Israelite monarchy** ([[context.israelite-monarchy]]) — the move
from tribal leadership to kingship, anointing, and the prophet–king relationship —
within the **covenant** framework that judges kings ([[context.covenant]]) and the
wider **ancient Near East** ([[context.ancient-near-east]]) of warfare and
court life. The period's history is debated; hold reconstructions with appropriate
confidence (see [[core.epistemic-humility]]).

## Literary features

Look for **contrast and foil** (Hannah/Eli, Samuel/Saul, Saul/David), **embedded
poetry**, **type-scenes**, **dramatic irony**, the ambivalent presentation of the
**request for a king**, repeated motifs (the LORD's choosing, the heart vs.
appearance), and dialogue that reveals character. Do not assume every feature is
present in a given passage; look for them.

## Key interpretive questions

Pose, do not answer:

- How does this episode advance the transition to monarchy, and how is kingship evaluated here?
- How does the narrator portray Samuel, Saul, or David — sympathetically, critically, or ambiguously?
- Is the passage reporting events or commending a response?
- What covenant or prophetic standard is being applied to the king?
- What would the ancient audience have assumed about anointing, warfare, or court life that a modern reader might miss?
- How do the embedded poems interpret the surrounding narrative?

## Common misreadings

- Reading the rise of David or the fall of Saul as simple hero-and-villain morality, missing the narrative's nuance.
- Treating narrated actions of kings as automatically endorsed or prescribed.
- Assigning characters motives the text does not state.
- Settling debated historical questions about early monarchy by assumption rather than observation.

## Handling uncertainty

The history of the early monarchy, the composition of Samuel, and the narrator's
exact evaluation of particular acts are debated. Present responsible readings,
distinguish what the text states from what is inferred, and label confidence
(see [[core.epistemic-humility]]); leave conclusions to the reader.
