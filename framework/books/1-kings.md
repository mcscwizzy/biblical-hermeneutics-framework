## Purpose

This module helps you approach 1 Kings as the narrative of Solomon, the temple,
and the division of the kingdom — kings measured against covenant fidelity, with
the prophets beginning to dominate. It is a hermeneutic profile, not a commentary:
it tells you what to notice and ask, assuming Core, Genre, and Context are already
loaded. It does not tell you what 1 Kings means.

## When to apply

When interpreting any passage in 1 Kings, on top of [[genre.narrative]] and the
world of the monarchy in [[context.israelite-monarchy]].

## Genre signals

Predominantly **narrative** ([[genre.narrative]]) organized by **regnal accounts**
with recurring evaluative formulae, interwoven with **temple-building** texts,
court history, and **prophetic** narratives (Elijah). Distinguish the
formulaic regnal frame from the extended story cycles.

## Historical anchors

The **monarchy** ([[context.israelite-monarchy]]) — Solomon's reign, the split
into northern and southern kingdoms, and their parallel histories — and the
**temple** ([[context.temple]]) as the center of worship, judged within the
**covenant** standard that the narrative applies to each king
([[context.covenant]]). The history and chronology are debated; hold them with
appropriate confidence (see [[core.epistemic-humility]]).

## Literary features

Look for the **regnal evaluation formulae** ("he did what was evil/right in the
eyes of the LORD"), the **synchronized** narration of the two kingdoms, the
**temple-dedication** material, **prophetic narrative cycles**, recurring motifs
(idolatry and the "high places," the word of the LORD fulfilled), and **contrast**
between kings. Do not assume every feature is present in a given passage; look for
them.

## Key interpretive questions

Pose, do not answer:

- How does this passage evaluate the king or kingdom against the covenant standard?
- Is the passage reporting events or commending a response?
- How do the regnal formulae shape the way the narrative judges?
- What role does the temple play in this section's concerns?
- How does the prophetic word function in relation to the kings here?
- What would the ancient audience have assumed about kingship, temple, or "high places" that a modern reader might miss?

## Common misreadings

- Reading the regnal accounts as neutral chronicle rather than as evaluated, selective narrative.
- Confusing the northern and southern kingdoms and their kings.
- Treating narrated royal actions as automatically endorsed or prescribed.
- Settling debated chronology and history by assumption rather than observation.

## Handling uncertainty

The chronology of the kings, the sources behind the regnal accounts, and the
book's composition are debated. Present the range, distinguish observation from
reconstruction, and label confidence (see [[core.epistemic-humility]]); leave
conclusions to the reader.
