## Purpose

This module helps you approach Ecclesiastes as reflective wisdom that probes the
limits of human understanding and the meaning of life "under the sun" — searching,
candid, and resistant to easy resolution. It is a hermeneutic profile, not a
commentary: it tells you what to notice and ask, assuming Core, Genre, and Context
are already loaded. It does not tell you what Ecclesiastes means.

## When to apply

When interpreting any passage in Ecclesiastes, on top of [[genre.wisdom]] (its
reflective, questioning mode) and the poetic sections in [[genre.poetry]], within
the broader wisdom conversation ([[context.wisdom-tradition]]).

## Genre signals

Reflective **wisdom** ([[genre.wisdom]]) in the voice of **Qoheleth** ("the
Teacher"), mixing first-person reflection, proverbial sayings, and **poetry**
([[genre.poetry]]), within a framing narrator's introduction and conclusion. Watch
for the distinction between Qoheleth's explorations and the frame that surrounds
them.

## Historical anchors

The **wisdom tradition** ([[context.wisdom-tradition]]) and its hardest questions —
the value of toil, the certainty of death, the limits of knowing — set against the
wider **ancient Near East** ([[context.ancient-near-east]]), which produced
comparable reflective and skeptical literature. Note both likeness and difference
without assuming borrowing. Date, authorship, and unity are debated; hold them
with appropriate confidence (see [[core.epistemic-humility]]).

## Literary features

Look for the keyword **hevel** ("vapor/breath/vanity") and its refrains, the
recurring "**under the sun**" and "**chasing wind**" phrases, the **frame
narrator** around Qoheleth's voice, **carpe-diem** "enjoyment" passages, sustained
**poetry** (e.g., "a time for everything"; the closing poem on aging), and the
tension the book holds without dissolving. Do not assume every feature is present
in a given passage; look for them.

## Key interpretive questions

Pose, do not answer:

- Is this the frame narrator's voice or Qoheleth's exploration, and how does that affect its weight?
- What does *hevel* mean in this context, and what range of renderings is defensible?
- Is the passage stating a settled conclusion or probing a tension?
- How do the "enjoyment" passages function within the book's larger argument?
- What would the ancient audience have assumed about toil, death, and wisdom that a modern reader might miss?
- How does the ending reframe (or not) what precedes it?

## Common misreadings

- Reading isolated "vanity" or "enjoyment" lines as the book's final word, apart from its movement.
- Flattening *hevel* to a single English gloss and building doctrine on it.
- Forcing a tidy resolution that erases the book's deliberate tension.
- Treating the frame narrator's voice and Qoheleth's explorations as indistinguishable.

## Handling uncertainty

Authorship, date, unity, the precise sense of *hevel*, and the relation of the
frame to Qoheleth's voice are debated, and the book intends to leave hard
questions open. Present the range, distinguish observation from reconstruction,
and label confidence (see [[core.epistemic-humility]]); leave conclusions to the
reader.
