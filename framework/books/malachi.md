## Purpose

This module helps you approach Malachi as post-exilic prophecy delivered in a
distinctive question-and-answer style — confronting a disillusioned community over
worship, faithfulness, and justice while pointing ahead. It is a hermeneutic
profile, not a commentary: it tells you what to notice and ask, assuming Core,
Genre, and Context are already loaded. It does not tell you what Malachi means.

## When to apply

When interpreting any passage in Malachi, on top of [[genre.prophecy]] and the
Persian-period restoration setting in [[context.exile-and-restoration]].

## Genre signals

Prophetic **oracles** ([[genre.prophecy]]) cast as a series of **disputations** —
an assertion, an imagined objection ("but you say…"), and a reply. The style is
largely **prose** argument rather than extended poetry. Track the back-and-forth
structure of each unit.

## Historical anchors

The **Persian-period restoration** ([[context.exile-and-restoration]]) — a settled
but disillusioned community, with a functioning **temple** and priesthood
([[context.temple]]) and questions about whether covenant faithfulness pays
([[context.covenant]]). Hold the historical details with appropriate confidence
(see [[core.epistemic-humility]]).

## Literary features

Look for the **disputation** pattern ("but you say…"), priestly and worship
concerns (blemished offerings, the priests' failure), the **covenant** framing
(including marriage and faithfulness), the **messenger** and **day-of-the-LORD**
passages, and reuse of earlier covenant and prophetic language
(see [[core.intertextuality]]). Do not assume every feature is present in a given
passage; look for them.

## Key interpretive questions

Pose, do not answer:

- What assertion, objection, and reply make up this disputation, and what is at stake?
- What worship, covenant, or justice failure is being confronted?
- How does the passage address the community's disillusionment or doubt?
- What do the messenger and day-of-the-LORD passages mean in their setting, and how are they later read?
- What would a post-exilic audience have assumed about temple, priesthood, or covenant that a modern reader might miss?

## Common misreadings

- Reading individual lines apart from the disputation structure that frames them.
- Treating the worship and marriage-faithfulness material through modern categories without its covenant setting.
- Reading the closing messenger passages as a detached timetable.
- Settling debated questions of the messenger's reference by assumption rather than observation.

## Handling uncertainty

The book's precise date, aspects of its composition, and the reference of the
messenger passages are debated and read differently across traditions. Present the
major approaches, distinguish observation from reconstruction, and label confidence
(see [[core.epistemic-humility]]); leave conclusions to the reader.
