## Purpose

This module helps you approach Revelation as a book that blends three genres and
draws constantly on earlier Scripture. It is a hermeneutic profile, not a
commentary: it tells you what to notice and ask, assuming Core, Genre, and
Context are already loaded. It does not tell you what Revelation means, and it
does not adjudicate between the major interpretive approaches.

## When to apply

When interpreting any passage in Revelation, on top of [[genre.apocalyptic]] and,
because the book is mixed, [[genre.prophecy]] and [[genre.epistle]].

## Genre signals

A **mixed** book. It is primarily **apocalyptic** ([[genre.apocalyptic]]) —
visionary, heavily symbolic — but it also presents itself as **prophecy**
([[genre.prophecy]]) and opens with an **epistolary** frame and seven letters to
churches ([[genre.epistle]]). Identify which mode is operating in a given
passage; do not read the whole book by one genre's rules.

## Historical anchors

The **Roman** imperial world ([[context.roman-empire]]) — emperor cult, power,
and the situation of the seven churches in Asia Minor — and the **Second Temple
Jewish** apocalyptic milieu ([[context.second-temple-judaism]]). Temple and
sacred-space imagery recurs ([[context.temple]]). Authorship, date, and setting
are debated; hold them with appropriate confidence
(see [[core.epistemic-humility]]).

## Literary features

Look for the dense reuse of **Old Testament imagery and allusion** (Revelation
rarely quotes but constantly echoes — see [[core.intertextuality]]), **symbolic
numbers, colors, and creatures**, **visionary structures** (sequences and
series), the distinction between what John **sees** and what he **hears** (the
two are sometimes interpreted against each other), **hymns** and liturgical
units, and **recapitulation** versus strict sequence. Do not assume every feature
governs a given passage; look for them.

## Key interpretive questions

Pose, do not answer:

- What Old Testament imagery is being reused, and how is it being redeployed here?
- What would the seven churches in their first-century setting have understood?
- In this passage, is the language operating symbolically, literally, or in a mixed way?
- Is John reporting what he *sees* or what he *hears*, and does that distinction matter here?
- What is the literary structure (sequence, series, recapitulation) doing?
- What is the effect on the audience (e.g., warning, assurance) beyond the imagery's details?

## Common misreadings

- Treating every symbol as a coded prediction of specific modern events.
- Reading the whole book by one genre's rules (e.g., as flat prophecy or flat letter).
- Ignoring the first-century churches actually addressed.
- Building a fixed chronological timetable from the visions, or setting dates.
- Decoding individual images while missing the passage's overall message.

## Handling uncertainty

Revelation is among the most contested books: authorship and date, structure
(sequence vs. recapitulation), the referents of symbols, and the major
interpretive approaches are all read very differently across traditions. Present
the major approaches neutrally, label confidence
(see [[core.epistemic-humility]]), and leave conclusions to the reader.
