## Purpose

This module helps you approach Ezekiel as the prophecy of a priest in exile —
strange visions, vivid symbolic acts, and oracles moving from judgment on
Jerusalem to the promise of restoration and a renewed temple. It is a hermeneutic
profile, not a commentary: it tells you what to notice and ask, assuming Core,
Genre, and Context are already loaded. It does not tell you what Ezekiel means.

## When to apply

When interpreting any passage in Ezekiel, on top of [[genre.prophecy]] and —
because of its visionary, symbol-laden material — [[genre.apocalyptic]].

## Genre signals

Prophetic **oracles** ([[genre.prophecy]]) combined with strongly visionary,
symbolic material that anticipates **apocalyptic** ([[genre.apocalyptic]]):
elaborate **vision** reports, **symbolic acts**, **allegories** and parables,
oracles **against the nations**, and the closing **temple vision**. Identify which
mode governs a given passage.

## Historical anchors

The **Babylonian exile** ([[context.exile-and-restoration]]) — Ezekiel prophesies
among the deportees — with the **temple** at the center of both judgment (the
glory departing) and hope (the glory returning to a renewed temple)
([[context.temple]]). Composition and the interpretation of the temple vision are
debated; hold them with appropriate confidence (see [[core.epistemic-humility]]).

## Literary features

Look for **dated oracles**, the recurring "**the word of the LORD came to me**" and
"**they will know that I am the LORD**" formulae, **vision** reports (the chariot,
the valley of dry bones), **symbolic acts** and **allegories**, the **departure
and return of the glory**, dense **priestly and temple** imagery, and heavy reuse
of earlier Scripture and influence on later apocalyptic
(see [[core.intertextuality]]). Do not assume every feature is present in a given
passage; look for them.

## Key interpretive questions

Pose, do not answer:

- Is this passage oracle, vision, symbolic act, or allegory — and how does that shape its claim?
- What is the exilic situation it addresses, so far as it can be discerned?
- Which images are symbolic, and what did they evoke for an exilic, priestly audience?
- How does the movement of the divine glory frame the book's message?
- What major approaches exist to the closing temple vision, and what are their trade-offs?
- How does the passage reuse earlier Scripture?

## Common misreadings

- Decoding visionary imagery (the chariot, the temple measurements) as a literal blueprint while missing the passage's message.
- Reading the symbolic acts as ordinary narrative rather than enacted prophecy.
- Treating the whole book by one genre's rules instead of identifying the mode.
- Settling the contested interpretation of the temple vision by assumption rather than weighing the approaches.

## Handling uncertainty

The book's composition, the function of its visionary numbers and measurements, and
above all the closing temple vision are read very differently across traditions.
Present the major approaches, distinguish observation from reconstruction, and
label confidence (see [[core.epistemic-humility]]); leave conclusions to the reader.
