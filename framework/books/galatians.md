## Purpose

This module helps you approach Galatians as Paul's urgent, polemical defense of his
gospel against pressure for Gentile believers to adopt the Jewish law —
particularly circumcision. It is a hermeneutic profile, not a commentary: it tells
you what to notice and ask, assuming Core, Genre, and Context are already loaded. It
does not tell you what Galatians means, and it does not adjudicate its famous
debates.

## When to apply

When interpreting any passage in Galatians, on top of [[genre.epistle]] and the
Second Temple Jewish matrix of its argument ([[context.second-temple-judaism]]).

## Genre signals

An **epistle** ([[genre.epistle]]), notably lacking the usual thanksgiving and
opening instead with rebuke — a **polemical**, rhetorically charged letter. It
combines **autobiographical narrative** (Paul's account of his calling and the
Jerusalem meeting), **scriptural argument**, and **ethical exhortation**.

## Historical anchors

The **Second Temple Jewish** world ([[context.second-temple-judaism]]) — law,
circumcision, covenant identity, and the terms of Gentile inclusion — within the
broader **Greco-Roman** setting of the Galatian churches
([[context.greco-roman-world]]). Pauline authorship is the scholarly consensus; the
destination ("north" vs. "south" Galatia), date, and the identity of Paul's
opponents are debated (see [[core.epistemic-humility]]).

## Literary features

Look for the **missing thanksgiving** and immediate rebuke, **autobiography** as
argument, sustained **Old Testament** exposition (Abraham, the law, the two
covenants — see [[core.intertextuality]]), **antitheses** (faith/works,
Spirit/flesh, freedom/slavery), the **fruit of the Spirit**, and **Greek** wording
bearing on contested phrases (see [[language.greek]]). Do not assume every feature
is present in a given passage; look for them.

## Key interpretive questions

Pose, do not answer:

- What is at stake in the conflict over law and circumcision for Gentile believers here?
- How does Paul use his autobiography or his Old Testament argument to make his case?
- Is Paul stating his own view, or characterizing the opponents' position?
- How does the passage fit the letter's polemical flow?
- What does a debated phrase (e.g., "works of the law," "faith of/in Christ") mean in context, and what are the options?
- How does the Jewish covenant background illuminate the passage?

## Common misreadings

- Reading isolated verses apart from the specific circumcision controversy Paul addresses.
- Importing a later theological system and reading it back into the letter.
- Resolving contested phrases by appeal to a tradition's doctrine rather than the argument and the evidence.
- Caricaturing the Jewish law or Paul's opponents instead of reading them in their setting.

## Handling uncertainty

Galatians is among the most debated letters: the destination and date, the meaning
of key phrases, and the nature of the opponents' teaching are read differently
across traditions. Present the major responsible interpretations and their basis,
label confidence (see [[core.epistemic-humility]]), and leave the conclusion to the
reader.
