## Purpose

This module helps you approach Obadiah, the shortest book in the Old Testament — a
single sustained oracle against Edom for its role in Jerusalem's fall, opening onto
the day of the LORD. It is a hermeneutic profile, not a commentary: it tells you
what to notice and ask, assuming Core, Genre, and Context are already loaded. It
does not tell you what Obadiah means.

## When to apply

When interpreting any passage in Obadiah, on top of [[genre.prophecy]] and its
compact Hebrew **poetry** ([[genre.poetry]]).

## Genre signals

A single **oracle against a nation** ([[genre.prophecy]]) in tight **poetry**
([[genre.poetry]]): indictment of Edom, announcement of its downfall, and a
closing turn to the day of the LORD and restoration for Zion. Read it as one
sustained argument rather than scattered sayings.

## Historical anchors

The book presupposes a catastrophe for Jerusalem in which **Edom** acted against
its kin — most naturally the Babylonian destruction, placing it in the **exile and
restoration** horizon ([[context.exile-and-restoration]]), though the precise date
is debated (see [[core.epistemic-humility]]). Edom's kinship with Israel (Esau and
Jacob) sharpens the offense.

## Literary features

Look for the **single-oracle** structure, the **indictment-then-sentence** pattern,
the **kinship** background (Edom as "brother"), the move from a specific nation to
the **day of the LORD** over all nations, and striking **overlap with an oracle in
Jeremiah** against Edom (see [[core.intertextuality]]). Do not assume every feature
is present in a given verse; look for them.

## Key interpretive questions

Pose, do not answer:

- What is Edom being indicted for, and how does the kinship background sharpen it?
- How does the oracle move from a specific nation to the day of the LORD?
- Which lines are figurative, and what did they evoke?
- What is the relationship between Obadiah and the parallel material in Jeremiah, and what approaches exist?
- What would the original audience, in the shadow of Jerusalem's fall, have understood that a modern reader might miss?

## Common misreadings

- Reading the oracle as generic anti-foreign sentiment rather than a response to a specific betrayal.
- Treating the verses as disconnected sayings rather than one sustained oracle.
- Settling the date and the relationship to Jeremiah by assumption rather than observation.
- Flattening the compact poetry into flat prose.

## Handling uncertainty

Obadiah's date, authorship, and its literary relationship to the parallel oracle in
Jeremiah are debated. Present the range, distinguish observation from
reconstruction, and label confidence (see [[core.epistemic-humility]]); leave
conclusions to the reader.
