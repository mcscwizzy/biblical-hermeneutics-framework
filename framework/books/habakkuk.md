## Purpose

This module helps you approach Habakkuk as a prophetic dialogue with God about
justice — the prophet questions, God answers, and the book closes in a psalm of
trust. It is a hermeneutic profile, not a commentary: it tells you what to notice
and ask, assuming Core, Genre, and Context are already loaded. It does not tell you
what Habakkuk means.

## When to apply

When interpreting any passage in Habakkuk, on top of [[genre.prophecy]] and its
Hebrew **poetry** ([[genre.poetry]]).

## Genre signals

An unusual prophetic book ([[genre.prophecy]]) shaped as a **dialogue** — the
prophet's **complaints** and God's **replies** — followed by a series of **woe**
oracles and a concluding **psalm/prayer** with musical notations
([[genre.poetry]]). Note that the prophet addresses God rather than only the people.

## Historical anchors

The rise of **Babylon** as the looming threat, in the last decades of the
**monarchy** ([[context.israelite-monarchy]]). The problem the book wrestles with —
why God would use a more violent nation as an instrument of judgment — is rooted in
that setting. Hold the historical details with appropriate confidence
(see [[core.epistemic-humility]]).

## Literary features

Look for the **complaint–response** structure, the theme of **the righteous living
by faithfulness**, the **woe** series against the oppressor, the closing
**theophany** psalm with its liturgical notes, and reuse of this material elsewhere
in Scripture (see [[core.intertextuality]]). Do not assume every feature is present
in a given passage; look for them.

## Key interpretive questions

Pose, do not answer:

- Is this the prophet's complaint, God's reply, a woe oracle, or the closing prayer — and how does that shape its claim?
- What is the problem of justice the passage is wrestling with?
- What does "the righteous will live by his faithfulness" mean in its context, and how is it later taken up?
- How does the closing psalm resolve (or hold open) the book's tension?
- What would the original audience have assumed about Babylon and divine justice that a modern reader might miss?

## Common misreadings

- Reading isolated famous lines apart from the dialogue and its movement.
- Treating the complaints as faithless rather than as a recognized form of honest address to God.
- Flattening the theophany psalm into flat prose.
- Settling the book's date and the identity of the oppressor by assumption rather than observation.

## Handling uncertainty

The book's date, the precise identity and role of the oppressor, and the rendering
of key lines are debated and read differently across traditions. Present the major
approaches, distinguish observation from reconstruction, and label confidence
(see [[core.epistemic-humility]]); leave conclusions to the reader.
