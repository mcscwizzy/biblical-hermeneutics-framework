## Purpose

This module helps you approach Joel as a prophetic call to repentance built around
a devastating locust plague and the looming "day of the LORD," moving toward
promise and the outpouring of the Spirit. It is a hermeneutic profile, not a
commentary: it tells you what to notice and ask, assuming Core, Genre, and Context
are already loaded. It does not tell you what Joel means.

## When to apply

When interpreting any passage in Joel, on top of [[genre.prophecy]] and its Hebrew
**poetry** ([[genre.poetry]]).

## Genre signals

Prophetic **poetry** ([[genre.poetry]], [[genre.prophecy]]) — a **summons to
lament and repent**, a vivid **locust/army** description, and oracles of the **day
of the LORD** and restoration. Watch how the imagery shifts between literal
plague and the larger day of the LORD.

## Historical anchors

The book gives few datable markers, and its setting is debated; this profile
locates it provisionally in the **post-exilic / restoration** horizon
([[context.exile-and-restoration]]), but the date is genuinely uncertain and
should be held loosely (see [[core.epistemic-humility]]). The day-of-the-LORD
theme connects it to the wider prophetic tradition.

## Literary features

Look for the **locust-plague** imagery and its possible blurring into an invading
**army**, the **day of the LORD** motif, the **call to communal lament and return**,
the **outpouring of the Spirit** passage, and the dense reuse of (and by) other
prophetic texts (see [[core.intertextuality]]). Do not assume every feature is
present in a given passage; look for them.

## Key interpretive questions

Pose, do not answer:

- Is the imagery here a literal locust plague, an army, the day of the LORD, or a deliberate blurring of these?
- What response is the passage summoning, and from whom?
- How does this unit move between judgment and promise?
- How is the "day of the LORD" functioning here within the wider prophetic use of the theme?
- How is this passage taken up later in Scripture, and how do traditions read that reuse?

## Common misreadings

- Fixing a confident date and setting the book has not made clear.
- Pressing the locust/army imagery into a single literal referent rather than weighing the poetry.
- Lifting the Spirit-outpouring passage from its context without observing its place in Joel.
- Reading the day-of-the-LORD language as a detached timetable.

## Handling uncertainty

Joel's date, setting, and the referent of its central imagery are debated. Present
the range, distinguish observation from reconstruction, and label confidence
(see [[core.epistemic-humility]]); leave conclusions to the reader.
