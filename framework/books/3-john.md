## Purpose

This module helps you approach 3 John as a brief personal letter about hospitality
and a leadership conflict — commending Gaius, confronting Diotrephes, and commending
Demetrius. It is a hermeneutic profile, not a commentary: it tells you what to notice
and ask, assuming Core, Genre, and Context are already loaded. It does not tell you
what 3 John means.

## When to apply

When interpreting any passage in 3 John, on top of [[genre.epistle]] and the social
conventions of hospitality and honor in the early churches
([[context.greco-roman-world]], [[context.patronage]]).

## Genre signals

A genuine, brief **personal letter** ([[genre.epistle]]) addressed to an individual
(Gaius), with a normal epistolary frame. It is the most "ordinary letter" in form
among the Johannine writings and turns on a concrete interpersonal and church
situation. Read it as a real occasional letter.

## Historical anchors

The conventions of **hospitality, patronage, and honor** ([[context.patronage]],
[[context.greco-roman-world]], [[context.honor-shame]]) — supporting traveling
workers, the honor of welcoming or refusing them, and the dynamics of standing and
rivalry in a local community. The exact relationships and the nature of the conflict
with Diotrephes are debated; hold them with appropriate confidence
(see [[core.epistemic-humility]]).

## Literary features

Look for the **epistolary frame**, the **commendation** of Gaius and of Demetrius,
the **rebuke** of Diotrephes (who "loves to be first" and refuses hospitality), the
language of **welcome and support** for traveling workers, and the honor/shame and
patronage dynamics throughout. Do not assume every feature is present in this short
letter; look for them.

## Key interpretive questions

Pose, do not answer:

- What concrete situation of hospitality and leadership is the letter addressing?
- What did supporting (or refusing) traveling workers mean in this setting?
- How do honor, status, and patronage illuminate the conflict with Diotrephes?
- What can be discerned about the relationships among the named figures, and how cautiously?
- What would the original recipients have assumed that a modern reader might miss?

## Common misreadings

- Reading the conflict with Diotrephes through modern church-office categories rather than its ancient honor and patronage setting.
- Pressing the brief, personal letter for systematic teaching it does not offer.
- Over-reconstructing the relationships and motives of the named figures.
- Treating the hospitality language apart from its social conventions.

## Handling uncertainty

The nature of the conflict, the roles of the figures named, and the relationship to
the other Johannine writings are debated. Present the major responsible
interpretations and their basis, label confidence (see [[core.epistemic-humility]]),
and leave the conclusion to the reader.
