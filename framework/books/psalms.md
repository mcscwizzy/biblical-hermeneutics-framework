## Purpose

This module helps you approach the Psalms as a collection of Hebrew poems used in
worship and prayer. It is a hermeneutic profile, not a commentary: it tells you
what to notice and ask, assuming Core, Genre, and Context are already loaded. It
does not tell you what a psalm means.

## When to apply

When interpreting any psalm or section of the Psalter, on top of
[[genre.poetry]] and, where relevant, the worship setting in [[context.temple]].

## Genre signals

Hebrew **poetry** throughout ([[genre.poetry]]), but in several recognizable
**psalm types** — for example lament, praise/hymn, thanksgiving, royal, wisdom,
and trust psalms. The Psalter is also an arranged **collection** (traditionally
five books) with superscriptions; the type and any heading shape how a psalm is
read. Identify the likely type before interpreting.

## Historical anchors

The world of Israelite **worship and the Temple** ([[context.temple]]) and the
broader **ancient Near East** ([[context.ancient-near-east]]) for shared poetic
imagery and royal/divine motifs. Superscriptions, authorship, and dating of
individual psalms are debated; treat them carefully
(see [[core.epistemic-humility]]).

## Literary features

Look for **parallelism** (the basic engine of Hebrew verse), **imagery** and
metaphor, **structure** (refrains, **acrostics**, **inclusio**), **shifts in
voice and address** (to God, self, congregation, enemies), **emotional movement**
(e.g., from lament to trust), and **quotations or echoes** of other Scripture
(see [[core.intertextuality]]). Do not assume every device is present in a given
psalm; look for them.

## Key interpretive questions

Pose, do not answer:

- What type of psalm is this, and what does that type characteristically do?
- Who is speaking, to whom, and how does the speaker's stance shift?
- Which images are figurative, and what did they evoke in their setting?
- How do parallelism and structure shape the psalm's movement and emphasis?
- What is the psalm's setting or use in worship, so far as it can be discerned?
- Does a superscription or placement in the collection affect the reading?

## Common misreadings

- Treating every poetic image as a literal or technical statement.
- Reading a single line of a parallel pair as an independent proposition.
- Mining individual verses for slogans apart from the psalm's overall movement.
- Assuming a superscription settles authorship, date, or occasion with certainty.
- Forcing a uniform structure onto poems of different types.

## Handling uncertainty

Authorship and dating, the meaning of technical terms in superscriptions, psalm
classification, and original liturgical settings are frequently uncertain.
Present the range, distinguish observation from reconstruction, and label
confidence (see [[core.epistemic-humility]]).
