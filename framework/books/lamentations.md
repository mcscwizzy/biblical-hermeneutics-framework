## Purpose

This module helps you approach Lamentations as a set of carefully crafted poems of
grief over the destruction of Jerusalem — not prophecy, but communal lament that
holds anguish, confession, and a thread of hope together. It is a hermeneutic
profile, not a commentary: it tells you what to notice and ask, assuming Core,
Genre, and Context are already loaded. It does not tell you what Lamentations means.

## When to apply

When interpreting any passage in Lamentations, on top of [[genre.poetry]] —
especially the lament form — and the catastrophe of the **exile**
([[context.exile-and-restoration]]).

## Genre signals

Hebrew **poetry** ([[genre.poetry]]) in the form of **communal and individual
lament** and **dirge** over a fallen city. It is *not* prophecy: it voices grief
and petition rather than announcing oracles. Four of its five poems are
**acrostics**, and the distinctive limping *qinah* (lament) rhythm shapes the
verse.

## Historical anchors

The aftermath of Jerusalem's fall and the **exile** ([[context.exile-and-restoration]])
— siege, destruction, and the loss of temple, city, and people. Authorship and
precise setting are debated; hold them with appropriate confidence
(see [[core.epistemic-humility]]).

## Literary features

Look for the **acrostic** structure (and its breaking), the **qinah** rhythm,
**personification** of the city as a grieving widow ("Daughter Zion"), shifts of
**voice** (the city, an observer, a sufferer, the community), the central turn
toward **hope** amid the grief, and the reuse of earlier Scripture's language of
covenant and judgment (see [[core.intertextuality]]). Do not assume every feature
is present in a given poem; look for them.

## Key interpretive questions

Pose, do not answer:

- Who is speaking in this section, and to whom is the grief or petition addressed?
- How does the acrostic structure shape the movement and containment of the grief?
- Which images are figurative (e.g., Zion as widow), and what did they evoke?
- How does the passage hold together anguish, confession, and hope?
- What would the original mourners have assumed about temple, city, and covenant that a modern reader might miss?
- How does the poem use earlier scriptural language of judgment?

## Common misreadings

- Reading Lamentations as prophecy or as detached theology rather than as voiced grief.
- Mining single lines (of either despair or hope) apart from each poem's movement.
- Flattening the poetry's figurative laments into flat statement.
- Rushing past the anguish to a resolution the poems themselves hold in tension.

## Handling uncertainty

Authorship, date, the setting of individual poems, and the structure's exact
function are debated. Present the range, distinguish observation from
reconstruction, and label confidence (see [[core.epistemic-humility]]); leave
conclusions to the reader.
