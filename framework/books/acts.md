## Purpose

This module helps you approach Acts as the second volume of Luke's work — a
narrative of the early movement's expansion from Jerusalem outward across the
Greco-Roman world, carried by the Spirit and by key figures. It is a hermeneutic
profile, not a commentary: it tells you what to notice and ask, assuming Core,
Genre, and Context are already loaded. It does not tell you what Acts means.

## When to apply

When interpreting any passage in Acts, on top of [[genre.narrative]] and the
Greco-Roman world it moves through ([[context.greco-roman-world]],
[[context.roman-empire]]).

## Genre signals

Predominantly **narrative** ([[genre.narrative]]) — historical narrative in the
ancient mode, the sequel to Luke — featuring extended **speeches** (a major
vehicle of its message), **summaries**, **travel** accounts, and first-person
"**we**" sections. Read the speeches and the narrative on their own terms.

## Historical anchors

The **Greco-Roman world** ([[context.greco-roman-world]], [[context.roman-empire]])
— cities, travel, citizenship, courts, synagogues, philosophy, and religion across
the empire — and **Second Temple Judaism** ([[context.second-temple-judaism]]) as
the matrix from which the movement spreads to Gentiles. Authorship, date, sources,
and the historical character of Acts are debated; hold them with appropriate
confidence (see [[core.epistemic-humility]]).

## Literary features

Look for the programmatic **outline** (Jerusalem to "the ends of the earth"), the
recurring **speeches** and their patterns, **parallels** between figures (e.g.,
Peter and Paul), the role of the **Spirit** and providential guidance, **summaries**
of community life, the **"we"** passages, **journey** structure, and the use of
earlier Scripture in the speeches (see [[core.intertextuality]]). Greek wording can
bear on meaning (see [[language.greek]]). Do not assume every feature is present in
a given passage; look for them.

## Key interpretive questions

Pose, do not answer:

- How does this passage advance the movement's expansion outward, and within Luke's larger design?
- Is the passage describing what happened or prescribing a norm — and how can the difference be discerned?
- What does a speech argue, to whom, and how does it use Scripture?
- How does the Greco-Roman urban and legal setting shape the episode?
- What would the original audience have assumed that a modern reader might miss?
- How does this connect back to the Gospel of Luke?

## Common misreadings

- Reading every narrated practice of the early community as a direct, binding command without weighing description versus prescription.
- Detaching Acts from its first volume, Luke.
- Treating the speeches as verbatim transcripts rather than as shaped, purposeful narration — a debated question to weigh.
- Settling the historical questions of Acts by assumption rather than observation.

## Handling uncertainty

Authorship, date, sources, the function of the "we" sections, and the historical
character of the speeches and events are debated. Present the range, separate
observation from reconstruction, and label confidence
(see [[core.epistemic-humility]]); leave conclusions to the reader.
