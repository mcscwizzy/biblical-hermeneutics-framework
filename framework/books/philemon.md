## Purpose

This module helps you approach Philemon, the shortest Pauline letter, as a delicate
personal appeal to a slave-owner about Onesimus — a letter that turns on persuasion,
relationship, and the social realities of slavery. It is a hermeneutic profile, not
a commentary: it tells you what to notice and ask, assuming Core, Genre, and Context
are already loaded. It does not tell you what Philemon means.

## When to apply

When interpreting any passage in Philemon, on top of [[genre.epistle]] and the
ancient institutions of **slavery and patronage** in [[context.roman-empire]] and
[[context.patronage]].

## Genre signals

A brief, personal **epistle** ([[genre.epistle]]) of **appeal and persuasion** — a
single sustained request rather than a treatise. It deploys the conventions of an
**intercession** letter and the language of **patronage, friendship, and honor**.
Read the whole as one carefully managed rhetorical appeal.

## Historical anchors

Greco-Roman **slavery** and the **household** ([[context.roman-empire]],
[[context.greco-roman-world]]), along with the conventions of **patronage and
intercession** ([[context.patronage]]) and **honor and obligation**
([[context.honor-shame]]). The exact situation — Onesimus's status and what Paul is
asking for — must be reconstructed and is debated; hold it with appropriate
confidence (see [[core.epistemic-humility]]).

## Literary features

Look for the **rhetoric of persuasion** (Paul appeals rather than commands), the
**wordplay** on Onesimus's name ("useful"), the language of **partnership and
debt** ("charge it to me"), the **honor and patronage** dynamics between Paul,
Philemon, and Onesimus, and the carefully managed **tact** of the whole. Do not
assume every feature is present in a given verse; look for them.

## Key interpretive questions

Pose, do not answer:

- What exactly is Paul asking Philemon to do, so far as it can be reconstructed?
- How do the conventions of patronage, intercession, and honor shape the appeal?
- How does Paul's rhetoric work by persuasion and relationship rather than command?
- What did slavery in this setting involve that a modern reader might misread?
- What major approaches exist to what the letter implies about slavery, and what are their trade-offs?

## Common misreadings

- Reading the letter through modern slavery and abolition debates without first engaging its ancient setting and the range of approaches.
- Over-reconstructing Onesimus's situation beyond what the letter supports.
- Missing the carefully managed rhetoric by reading the appeal as a flat command.
- Drawing sweeping conclusions about the New Testament and slavery from this brief, occasional letter alone.

## Handling uncertainty

Onesimus's exact situation, what Paul ultimately requests, and what the letter
implies about slavery are debated and read very differently across traditions.
Present the major responsible interpretations and their basis, label confidence
(see [[core.epistemic-humility]]), and leave the conclusion to the reader.
