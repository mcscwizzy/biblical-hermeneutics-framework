## Purpose

This module helps you approach Genesis as the opening narrative of the Torah,
read in its ancient Near Eastern world. It is a hermeneutic profile, not a
commentary: it tells you what to notice and ask, assuming Core, Genre, and
Context are already loaded. It does not tell you what Genesis means.

## When to apply

When interpreting any passage in Genesis, on top of [[genre.narrative]] and the
ancient Near Eastern background in [[context.ancient-near-east]].

## Genre signals

Predominantly **narrative** ([[genre.narrative]]), including the primeval
accounts (often distinguished from the ancestral narratives that follow).
Embedded forms appear throughout: **genealogies**, **poetry** and blessings
(e.g., poetic oracles within the prose), and occasional etiological notes. Treat
genealogies and poetic units according to their own conventions.

## Historical anchors

The **ancient Near East** ([[context.ancient-near-east]]) — cosmology, kingship,
flood and creation traditions, treaty/**covenant** forms ([[context.covenant]]),
and family/clan and inheritance structures. Sacred-space and presence themes
connect to [[context.temple]]. Authorship, date, and compositional history are
debated; hold them with appropriate confidence
(see [[core.epistemic-humility]]).

## Literary features

Look for **repeated words and motifs** (e.g., blessing, seed/offspring, land),
**structuring formulae** (the recurring "these are the generations" headings),
**type-scenes** and repeated narrative patterns, **genealogies**, **inclusio**
and **chiasm**, **irony**, and **wordplay** in names — and the dense **inner-
biblical echoes** Genesis later receives (see [[core.intertextuality]]). Do not
assume every feature is present in a given passage; look for them.

## Key interpretive questions

Pose, do not answer:

- Why is this episode being told, and how does it function in the larger narrative?
- What would Genesis's ancient audience have assumed that a modern reader might miss?
- Is this passage reporting (descriptive) or commending (prescriptive)?
- What covenant or blessing theme is being introduced or advanced here?
- How do the genealogies and structuring formulae shape the section?
- How does the narrator evaluate the characters' actions, if at all?

## Common misreadings

- Importing modern scientific debates and reading them back into the text before
  observing what the passage itself says and does.
- Treating narrated actions of characters as automatically endorsed or prescribed.
- Reading genealogies or numbers as if they served modern record-keeping aims
  without asking their ancient function.
- Flattening poetic blessings into flat prose, or allegorizing narrative detail.
- Settling debated origins questions by assumption rather than observation.

## Handling uncertainty

Authorship and composition, the genre and function of the primeval accounts,
chronology and numbers, and the relationship to ANE parallels are all debated and
tradition-linked. Present the range, separate observation from reconstruction,
and label confidence (see [[core.epistemic-humility]]); leave conclusions to the
reader.
