## Purpose

This module helps you approach Hebrews as a sustained sermon — a "word of
exhortation" — that argues from the Jewish Scriptures for the superiority and
sufficiency of Christ, urging a pressured community not to fall away. It is a
hermeneutic profile, not a commentary: it tells you what to notice and ask,
assuming Core, Genre, and Context are already loaded. It does not tell you what
Hebrews means.

## When to apply

When interpreting any passage in Hebrews, on top of [[genre.epistle]] — read as a
written homily — and the temple/sacrificial world of [[context.temple]] and
[[context.second-temple-judaism]].

## Genre signals

More a **written sermon** than a typical letter — it calls itself a "word of
exhortation" ([[genre.epistle]] in a homiletic mode) and ends with letter-like
greetings. It alternates sustained **scriptural exposition** with urgent
**exhortation/warning** sections. Read exposition and warning according to their
distinct functions.

## Historical anchors

The **temple and sacrificial system** ([[context.temple]]) — priesthood,
sacrifice, covenant, tabernacle — and the **Second Temple Jewish** thought world
([[context.second-temple-judaism]]) are the air the argument breathes. Authorship
is unknown and was debated in the early church; date and audience are also debated
(see [[core.epistemic-humility]]).

## Literary features

Look for the alternation of **exposition and exhortation**, the chain of **Old
Testament quotations** and sustained interpretation (Psalms, the Melchizedek and
tabernacle texts — see [[core.intertextuality]]), **"better/superior"** comparisons,
the **warning passages**, the **"by faith"** catalogue, **typology** (earthly
shadow and heavenly reality), and **Greek** wording bearing on meaning
(see [[language.greek]]). Do not assume every feature is present in a given
passage; look for them.

## Key interpretive questions

Pose, do not answer:

- Is this passage scriptural exposition or exhortation/warning, and how does that shape its claim?
- What Old Testament text is being expounded, and how does Hebrews use it?
- How does the temple and sacrificial background illuminate the argument here?
- How does the comparison ("better than…") function in the flow of the sermon?
- What major approaches exist to the warning passages, and what are their trade-offs?
- What would a pressured first-century audience have heard that a modern reader might miss?

## Common misreadings

- Reading the warning passages in isolation to settle debated questions rather than weighing the approaches.
- Treating the Old Testament citations without attention to their source context and the way Hebrews reads them.
- Reading the book as a flat letter and missing its character as a sermon.
- Pressing the typology into a system the text does not assert.

## Handling uncertainty

Authorship, date, audience, and the force of the warning passages are debated and
read very differently across traditions. Present the major responsible
interpretations and their basis, label confidence (see [[core.epistemic-humility]]),
and leave the conclusion to the reader.
