## Purpose

This module helps you approach Job as a wisdom book that probes the limits of the
usual wisdom answers — innocent suffering, divine justice, and the right to
question God — through an extended poetic debate. It is a hermeneutic profile, not
a commentary: it tells you what to notice and ask, assuming Core, Genre, and
Context are already loaded. It does not tell you what Job means.

## When to apply

When interpreting any passage in Job, on top of [[genre.wisdom]] (especially its
reflective, probing mode) and [[genre.poetry]], within the broader conversation of
the wisdom tradition ([[context.wisdom-tradition]]).

## Genre signals

A **prose frame** (prologue and epilogue) enclosing a long **poetic debate**
([[genre.poetry]]) — cycles of speeches between Job and his friends, the Elihu
speeches, and the divine speeches from the whirlwind. This is reflective or
"protest" **wisdom** ([[genre.wisdom]]). Whose voice is speaking, and where they
stand in the argument, governs how a line should be weighed.

## Historical anchors

The **wisdom tradition** ([[context.wisdom-tradition]]) and its debate over whether
the world reliably rewards the righteous, set against the wider **ancient Near
East** ([[context.ancient-near-east]]), which produced comparable reflections on
suffering and justice. Note both likeness and difference without assuming
borrowing. Date, setting, and the book's composition are debated; hold them with
appropriate confidence (see [[core.epistemic-humility]]).

## Literary features

Look for the **prose–poetry frame**, the **speech cycles** and their structure,
the gap between what the reader knows (the prologue) and what the characters know,
**legal and courtroom** imagery (Job's "case"), dense **nature imagery** in the
divine speeches, **irony**, and the way the **friends voice conventional wisdom**
that the book itself tests. Do not assume every feature is present in a given
passage; look for them.

## Key interpretive questions

Pose, do not answer:

- Who is speaking here, and how does the book itself evaluate their position?
- Does this speech advance, defend, or critique conventional wisdom about suffering?
- What does the reader know (from the prologue) that the characters do not, and how does that shape the passage?
- Is the passage stating the book's own view, or a view it is examining?
- What would the ancient audience have assumed about justice, suffering, and the divine that a modern reader might miss?
- What do the divine speeches actually address, and what do they leave open?

## Common misreadings

- Quoting the friends' speeches as the book's own teaching, missing that the book tests them.
- Forcing a tidy resolution that blunts the book's hard questions.
- Treating the prose frame and the poetic debate by one set of rules.
- Reading isolated lines as freestanding doctrine apart from the speaker and the argument.

## Handling uncertainty

The book's composition (e.g., the Elihu speeches), its date and setting, the
meaning of rare words, and how to read the ending are debated, and the book
deliberately leaves hard questions open. Present the range, distinguish observation
from reconstruction, and label confidence (see [[core.epistemic-humility]]); leave
conclusions to the reader.
