## Purpose

This module helps you approach Exodus as a narrative that turns into law and
worship: deliverance from Egypt, covenant at Sinai, and instructions for the
tabernacle. It is a hermeneutic profile, not a commentary: it tells you what to
notice and ask, assuming Core, Genre, and Context are already loaded. It does not
tell you what Exodus means.

## When to apply

When interpreting any passage in Exodus, on top of [[genre.narrative]] and —
because roughly the second half shifts into covenant stipulations and ritual
instruction — [[genre.law]].

## Genre signals

A **mixed** book. The first part is sustained **narrative** ([[genre.narrative]])
— oppression, deliverance, and journey. From Sinai onward it becomes **law and
covenant** ([[genre.law]]): the Ten Words, the case law of the Covenant Code, and
detailed **tabernacle instructions** (given, then carried out). Embedded **poetry**
appears (e.g., the Song of the Sea). Identify which mode is operating in a given
passage.

## Historical anchors

The **ancient Near East** ([[context.ancient-near-east]]) — Egypt, slavery,
plagues against the backdrop of ANE religion, and the **treaty/covenant** forms
behind Sinai ([[context.covenant]]). The tabernacle connects to sacred-space and
presence themes ([[context.temple]]). Date, route, and the historical questions
of the exodus are debated; hold them with appropriate confidence
(see [[core.epistemic-humility]]).

## Literary features

Look for the **deliverance plot** and its reversals, **the LORD's self-revelation**
(the divine name, "that you may know…"), the **plague sequence** and its
patterning, **repetition** in the tabernacle texts (command then fulfillment),
embedded **poetry**, **wordplay** in names, and the dense **inner-biblical echoes**
the exodus story later generates (see [[core.intertextuality]]). Do not assume
every feature is present in a given passage; look for them.

## Key interpretive questions

Pose, do not answer:

- How does this episode advance the movement from slavery to covenant and worship?
- What is being revealed about God's character and name here, and to whom?
- Is the passage narrating events, giving law, or prescribing worship — and how does that shape its claim on the reader?
- What would Exodus's ancient audience have assumed that a modern reader might miss?
- How do the tabernacle instructions and their fulfillment function in the book?
- How is this material later reused within Scripture?

## Common misreadings

- Treating the narrative portions and the legal/ritual portions by one set of rules.
- Reading the plagues or the sea crossing primarily as puzzles to harmonize with science before observing the text's own emphasis.
- Allegorizing tabernacle details into a code while missing their stated function.
- Assuming narrated actions are automatically endorsed or prescribed.
- Settling debated historical questions (date, route) by assumption rather than observation.

## Handling uncertainty

The date and route of the exodus, the relationship to Egyptian and ANE history,
and the composition of the legal and tabernacle material are debated and
tradition-linked. Present the range, separate observation from reconstruction,
and label confidence (see [[core.epistemic-humility]]); leave conclusions to the
reader.
