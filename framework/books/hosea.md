## Purpose

This module helps you approach Hosea as prophecy to the northern kingdom in its
last decades — covenant unfaithfulness dramatized through the prophet's own
marriage, with judgment and tenacious love held together. It is a hermeneutic
profile, not a commentary: it tells you what to notice and ask, assuming Core,
Genre, and Context are already loaded. It does not tell you what Hosea means.

## When to apply

When interpreting any passage in Hosea, on top of [[genre.prophecy]] and the
Hebrew **poetry** that carries most of it ([[genre.poetry]]).

## Genre signals

Prophetic **oracles** ([[genre.prophecy]]) in dense, sometimes difficult **poetry**
([[genre.poetry]]), framed by the **sign-act** of the prophet's marriage and
children. Identify whether a unit is judgment, accusation, or promise of
restoration.

## Historical anchors

The **northern kingdom** (Israel/Ephraim) in the eighth century under Assyrian
threat ([[context.israelite-monarchy]]), read against the **covenant** bond whose
betrayal the book likens to marital unfaithfulness ([[context.covenant]]). The
text is among the most textually difficult in the prophets; hold reconstructions
with appropriate confidence (see [[core.epistemic-humility]]).

## Literary features

Look for the **marriage/adultery metaphor** for covenant, the **symbolic names**
of the children, recurring imagery (Israel as faithless spouse, wayward child,
unresponsive plant), abrupt **shifts between judgment and tenderness**, and reuse
of earlier covenant and exodus traditions (see [[core.intertextuality]]). Do not
assume every feature is present in a given passage; look for them.

## Key interpretive questions

Pose, do not answer:

- What covenant accusation, judgment, or promise is this passage making, and to whom?
- How does the marriage metaphor shape the passage, and where are its limits as a figure?
- Which images are figurative, and what did they evoke for an eighth-century northern audience?
- How does the passage hold judgment and steadfast love together?
- What would the original audience have assumed about marriage, covenant, or Assyria that a modern reader might miss?

## Common misreadings

- Pressing the marriage metaphor beyond its point, or reading the sign-act marriage as a simple model for relationships.
- Flattening difficult poetry into flat prose, or resolving its textual hard spots by assertion.
- Lifting tender promises out of their covenant-judgment setting.
- Treating the symbolic names as incidental rather than as part of the message.

## Handling uncertainty

The book's text is notoriously difficult, and the nature of the marriage material
and the dating of units are debated. Present the range, distinguish observation
from reconstruction, and label confidence (see [[core.epistemic-humility]]); leave
conclusions to the reader.
