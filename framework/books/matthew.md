## Purpose

This module helps you approach Matthew as one of the four Gospels, attentive to
its distinct shaping and its dense engagement with Jewish Scripture. It is a
hermeneutic profile, not a commentary: it tells you what to notice and ask,
assuming Core, Genre, and Context are already loaded. It does not tell you what
Matthew means.

## When to apply

When interpreting any passage in Matthew, on top of [[genre.gospel]] and the
Jewish world of [[context.second-temple-judaism]].

## Genre signals

A **Gospel** ([[genre.gospel]]) — theological narrative in the form of ancient
biography — combining **narrative** ([[genre.narrative]]) with large **teaching
discourses** (Matthew notably gathers Jesus's teaching into extended blocks).
Embedded forms include **parables**, **sayings**, **genealogy**, and
**fulfillment formulae**. Read narrative and discourse on their own terms.

## Historical anchors

**Second Temple Judaism** ([[context.second-temple-judaism]]) — synagogue,
Scripture, law, sabbath, purity, and messianic expectation — and the
**Roman** provincial setting ([[context.roman-empire]]). Matthew's audience,
authorship, date, and relationship to the other Gospels are debated; hold them
with appropriate confidence (see [[core.epistemic-humility]]).

## Literary features

Look for **fulfillment quotations** and heavy **Old Testament allusion**
(see [[core.intertextuality]]), the **structured discourses** and their narrative
frames, **repeated formulae** marking transitions, **inclusio** (e.g., framing
motifs across the book), **typology and echoes** of Israel's story, **Greek**
wording that bears on meaning (see [[language.greek]]), and characterization of
groups and disciples. Do not assume every feature is present in a given passage;
look for them.

## Key interpretive questions

Pose, do not answer:

- What earlier Scripture is being quoted or echoed, and how does Matthew use it?
- How does this passage sit within the surrounding narrative or discourse block?
- What would a Jewish audience in Matthew's setting hear that a modern reader might miss?
- How does Matthew's presentation differ from the parallel accounts, and what does that emphasize?
- Is a saying addressed to a specific situation, and how is it framed for the reader?
- How does the Greek wording shape a contested phrase?

## Common misreadings

- Harmonizing the Gospels in a way that erases Matthew's distinct emphasis.
- Reading a fulfillment quotation without attending to its source context.
- Allegorizing the details of parables rather than seeking their point in context.
- Caricaturing the Jewish groups in the narrative instead of reading them in their setting.
- Treating every narrated act as a direct command for readers today.

## Handling uncertainty

Authorship, date, audience, sources and the relationship among the Gospels, and
the referents of some sayings are debated. Present the range, separate
observation from reconstruction, and label confidence
(see [[core.epistemic-humility]]).
