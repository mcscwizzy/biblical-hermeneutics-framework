## Purpose

This module helps you approach Isaiah as a large prophetic book that spans
judgment and comfort — addressing Judah under threat and the hope of restoration
beyond exile — in some of Scripture's richest poetry. It is a hermeneutic profile,
not a commentary: it tells you what to notice and ask, assuming Core, Genre, and
Context are already loaded. It does not tell you what Isaiah means.

## When to apply

When interpreting any passage in Isaiah, on top of [[genre.prophecy]] and the
Hebrew **poetry** in which much of it is cast ([[genre.poetry]]).

## Genre signals

Predominantly prophetic **oracles** ([[genre.prophecy]]) in **poetry**
([[genre.poetry]]), with stretches of **narrative** (e.g., the Hezekiah accounts),
**vision** reports, **woe** and **judgment** oracles, oracles **against the
nations**, and songs (e.g., the vineyard song, the Servant songs). Identify which
form is operating in a given passage.

## Historical anchors

The book reaches across eras: the **Israelite monarchy** under Assyrian threat
([[context.israelite-monarchy]]) and the horizon of Babylonian **exile and
restoration** ([[context.exile-and-restoration]]). Authorship and the book's
composition (including the unity of its parts) are heavily debated and
tradition-linked; hold them with appropriate confidence
(see [[core.epistemic-humility]]).

## Literary features

Look for **judgment-and-salvation** patterning, **oracles against the nations**,
the **Servant** passages, vivid **imagery** (highway, vineyard, new creation),
**parallelism** and other poetic devices, recurring motifs (the Holy One of
Israel, remnant, Zion), and the dense web of **inner-biblical reuse** Isaiah both
draws on and later generates (see [[core.intertextuality]]). Do not assume every
feature is present in a given passage; look for them.

## Key interpretive questions

Pose, do not answer:

- What is the historical situation this oracle addresses, so far as it can be discerned?
- Is the passage announcing judgment, promising restoration, or both — and to whom?
- Which images are figurative, and what did they evoke in their setting?
- How do the parts of the book (and their different horizons) relate here?
- How is this passage taken up later in Scripture, and how do traditions differ on its reference?
- What would the original audience have understood that a modern reader might miss?

## Common misreadings

- Reading oracles as detached predictions while ignoring the situation they first addressed.
- Flattening poetry into flat prose, or pressing figurative imagery as technical description.
- Settling the contested authorship and composition questions by assumption rather than observation.
- Assuming one tradition's reading of a passage (e.g., the Servant songs) is the only possible one.

## Handling uncertainty

The book's authorship and composition, the dating of its parts, and the reference
of key passages (e.g., the Servant songs, child-birth oracles) are debated and
read differently across traditions. Present the major approaches, distinguish
observation from reconstruction, and label confidence
(see [[core.epistemic-humility]]); leave conclusions to the reader.
