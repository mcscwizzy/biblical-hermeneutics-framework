## Purpose

This module helps you approach Nahum as a sustained poetic oracle announcing the
fall of Nineveh — the violent Assyrian capital — read as both judgment on an
oppressor and comfort for its victims. It is a hermeneutic profile, not a
commentary: it tells you what to notice and ask, assuming Core, Genre, and Context
are already loaded. It does not tell you what Nahum means.

## When to apply

When interpreting any passage in Nahum, on top of [[genre.prophecy]] and its vivid
Hebrew **poetry** ([[genre.poetry]]).

## Genre signals

A sustained **oracle against a nation** ([[genre.prophecy]]) in some of the most
vivid **poetry** ([[genre.poetry]]) in the prophets: a partial opening **acrostic**
hymn, then dramatic battle imagery of Nineveh's downfall. Read it as poetry whose
force lies in its imagery and sound.

## Historical anchors

The later years of the **Assyrian** empire before Nineveh's fall, within the
**monarchy** period under Assyrian shadow ([[context.israelite-monarchy]]). Assyria
was remembered for extreme cruelty, which frames the book's tone. Hold the
historical details with appropriate confidence (see [[core.epistemic-humility]]).

## Literary features

Look for the opening **acrostic** hymn of the divine warrior, **rapid battle
imagery** (chariots, sword, siege), **taunt** and **woe** forms, vivid **sound and
movement** in the verse, and reuse of shared traditions of God as warrior and judge
(see [[core.intertextuality]]). Do not assume every feature is present in a given
passage; look for them.

## Key interpretive questions

Pose, do not answer:

- What is Nineveh being judged for, and how does the book frame that judgment?
- How does the vivid battle imagery work as poetry rather than literal report?
- How does the same message function as judgment on an oppressor and comfort to its victims?
- What did the audience know of Assyria's cruelty that shapes the book's tone?
- How does the passage draw on shared imagery of the divine warrior?

## Common misreadings

- Reading Nahum as mere nationalistic gloating rather than as judgment on cruelty and comfort for the oppressed.
- Pressing the battle imagery into literal prediction while missing the poetry.
- Reading the book in isolation from the question of divine justice toward oppressors.
- Flattening the sound-rich verse into flat prose.

## Handling uncertainty

The book's precise date, the extent of the opening acrostic, and aspects of its
composition are debated. Present the range, distinguish observation from
reconstruction, and label confidence (see [[core.epistemic-humility]]); leave
conclusions to the reader.
