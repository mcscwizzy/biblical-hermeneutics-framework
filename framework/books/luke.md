## Purpose

This module helps you approach Luke as an orderly, carefully researched Gospel —
the first volume of a two-part work with Acts — attentive to the marginalized, to
prayer and the Spirit, and to a wide Greco-Roman readership. It is a hermeneutic
profile, not a commentary: it tells you what to notice and ask, assuming Core,
Genre, and Context are already loaded. It does not tell you what Luke means.

## When to apply

When interpreting any passage in Luke, on top of [[genre.gospel]], the Jewish world
of [[context.second-temple-judaism]], and its Greco-Roman setting
([[context.greco-roman-world]]).

## Genre signals

A **Gospel** ([[genre.gospel]]) — theological **narrative** ([[genre.narrative]])
in the form of ancient biography — opening with a formal **historiographic
preface** and conceived as the first part of **Luke–Acts**. Embedded forms include
**parables** (many unique to Luke), **hymns/canticles**, **travel-narrative**
material, and meal scenes. Read narrative and its embedded forms on their own terms.

## Historical anchors

**Second Temple Judaism** ([[context.second-temple-judaism]]) and the wider
**Greco-Roman world** ([[context.greco-roman-world]], [[context.roman-empire]]) —
empire, social status, wealth and poverty, and a likely Gentile-inclusive
audience. Authorship, date, sources, and the Gospels' relationship are debated;
hold them with appropriate confidence (see [[core.epistemic-humility]]).

## Literary features

Look for the **preface** and orderly arrangement, the **infancy** narrative and its
**canticles**, the extended **travel narrative** to Jerusalem, **parables** unique
to Luke, recurring emphases (the **poor and outsiders**, **women**, **prayer**, the
**Spirit**, table fellowship, reversal), connections forward to **Acts**, and the
use of earlier Scripture (see [[core.intertextuality]]). Greek wording can bear on
meaning (see [[language.greek]]). Do not assume every feature is present in a given
passage; look for them.

## Key interpretive questions

Pose, do not answer:

- How does this passage fit Luke's ordered design and its links to Acts?
- What emphasis (the marginalized, prayer, the Spirit, reversal) is at work here?
- How does the Greco-Roman social setting shape what the passage assumes?
- What would Luke's audience hear that a modern reader might miss?
- How does Luke's version differ from the parallels, and what does that emphasize?
- How does the passage use earlier Scripture?

## Common misreadings

- Harmonizing the Gospels in a way that erases Luke's distinct emphases.
- Reading Luke without regard to its continuation in Acts.
- Allegorizing the details of parables rather than seeking their point in context.
- Treating every narrated act as a direct command for readers today.

## Handling uncertainty

Authorship, date, sources, and the relationship among the Gospels are debated.
Present the range, separate observation from reconstruction, and label confidence
(see [[core.epistemic-humility]]); leave conclusions to the reader.
