## Purpose

This module helps you approach Proverbs as practical wisdom — concise, poetic
sayings and longer instructions about living skillfully in God's world, grounded
in "the fear of the LORD." It is a hermeneutic profile, not a commentary: it tells
you what to notice and ask, assuming Core, Genre, and Context are already loaded.
It does not tell you what Proverbs means.

## When to apply

When interpreting any passage in Proverbs, on top of [[genre.wisdom]] (its
practical mode) and the poetic form in [[genre.poetry]], within the broader wisdom
conversation ([[context.wisdom-tradition]]).

## Genre signals

Two main forms: extended **instructions** (the longer father-to-son discourses,
including the personified figures of Wisdom and Folly) and the **proverb proper**
(short, poetic sayings, mostly two-line). Both are **wisdom** ([[genre.wisdom]]) in
**poetic** form ([[genre.poetry]]). Read a saying as a memorable, generally true
observation, not an unconditional promise or law.

## Historical anchors

The **wisdom tradition** ([[context.wisdom-tradition]]) of training character and
skill for life, set against the wider **ancient Near East**
([[context.ancient-near-east]]), which produced comparable instruction literature
(note both likeness and difference, without assuming borrowing). The collection's
date and composite headings are debated; hold them with appropriate confidence
(see [[core.epistemic-humility]]).

## Literary features

Look for **parallelism** (synonymous, antithetic, and other patterns — the engine
of the saying), **imagery** and metaphor, the **personification** of Wisdom and
Folly, **better-than** and **numerical** sayings, **collections** with their own
headings, the **acrostic** poem on the capable woman, and the recurring frame of
the **fear of the LORD**. Do not assume every feature is present in a given
passage; look for them.

## Key interpretive questions

Pose, do not answer:

- Is this an extended instruction or a stand-alone saying, and how does that shape its claim?
- What general pattern of life is the saying observing — and what are its limits?
- How does parallelism shape the meaning of the two lines together?
- Which images or personifications are at work, and what did they evoke?
- How does this passage relate to "the fear of the LORD" that frames the book?
- What would the ancient audience have assumed that a modern reader might miss?

## Common misreadings

- Reading proverbs as unconditional promises or guarantees, then treating exceptions as failures.
- Stringing isolated sayings into a system the collection does not assert.
- Reading one line of a parallel pair as an independent proposition.
- Flattening vivid imagery and personification into flat prose.

## Handling uncertainty

The date and growth of the collection, the headings, the rendering of rare words,
and how far a given saying generalizes are debated, and proverbs are deliberately
context-sensitive. Present the range, distinguish observation from reconstruction,
and label confidence (see [[core.epistemic-humility]]); leave conclusions to the
reader.
