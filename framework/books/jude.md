## Purpose

This module helps you approach Jude as a short, urgent letter calling readers to
"contend for the faith" against intruding false teachers — densely woven from Jewish
Scripture and tradition. It is a hermeneutic profile, not a commentary: it tells you
what to notice and ask, assuming Core, Genre, and Context are already loaded. It does
not tell you what Jude means.

## When to apply

When interpreting any passage in Jude, on top of [[genre.epistle]] and the Second
Temple Jewish traditions it draws on so heavily ([[context.second-temple-judaism]]).

## Genre signals

A brief **epistle** ([[genre.epistle]]) that is almost entirely **polemic** — an
appeal to resist false teachers, built from a chain of **examples** of judgment. Its
middle section closely **parallels 2 Peter**. Read it as one sustained, example-laden
exhortation.

## Historical anchors

The **Second Temple Jewish** world ([[context.second-temple-judaism]]) is essential:
Jude draws on Old Testament episodes **and on Jewish traditions outside the Hebrew
canon** — notably alluding to **1 Enoch** and apparently to a tradition about the
body of Moses (associated with the Assumption of Moses). Observe this as a feature of
how Jude argues; how it bears on questions of canon and inspiration is read
differently across traditions and is not settled here (see [[core.epistemic-humility]]).

## Literary features

Look for the **catena of judgment examples** (often in triads), the dense reuse of
**Old Testament** and **Second Temple Jewish** sources, the explicit **citation of 1
Enoch** and allusion to extra-canonical tradition (see [[core.intertextuality]]),
vivid **metaphors** for the false teachers, and the closing **doxology**; **Greek**
wording can bear on meaning (see [[language.greek]]). Do not assume every feature is
present in a given verse; look for them.

## Key interpretive questions

Pose, do not answer:

- What is the false teaching or conduct Jude is countering, so far as it can be discerned?
- How does each judgment example function in the argument?
- How is Jude using its sources — both Old Testament and extra-canonical Jewish tradition — and how do traditions read that use?
- How does Jude relate to the parallel material in 2 Peter, and what approaches exist to that relationship?
- What would the original audience have recognized in these allusions that a modern reader might miss?

## Common misreadings

- Treating Jude's use of 1 Enoch and other extra-canonical traditions as a settled verdict on canon or inspiration, in either direction, rather than observing it and noting the range of approaches.
- Reading the judgment examples without recognizing the Jewish traditions behind them.
- Treating the parallels with 2 Peter without asking about their literary relationship.
- Reading the polemic as detached invective apart from its appeal to "contend for the faith."

## Handling uncertainty

Authorship, date, the direction of the relationship with 2 Peter, and how to weigh
Jude's use of extra-canonical sources are debated and read very differently across
traditions. Present the major responsible interpretations and their basis, label
confidence (see [[core.epistemic-humility]]), and leave the conclusion to the reader.
