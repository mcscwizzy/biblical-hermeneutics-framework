## Purpose

This module helps you approach 2 Corinthians as Paul's most personal and impassioned
letter — a defense of his ministry amid strained relations and rival teachers,
redefining strength through weakness. It is a hermeneutic profile, not a commentary:
it tells you what to notice and ask, assuming Core, Genre, and Context are already
loaded. It does not tell you what 2 Corinthians means.

## When to apply

When interpreting any passage in 2 Corinthians, on top of [[genre.epistle]] and the
honor-conscious Greco-Roman world of [[context.honor-shame]] and
[[context.greco-roman-world]].

## Genre signals

An **epistle** ([[genre.epistle]]) marked by **apology** (self-defense),
**emotional appeal**, and **irony** — including the so-called "fool's speech" of
boasting. The letter's tone shifts sharply between sections; many scholars discuss
whether it combines more than one letter. Read each section attentive to its tone
and aim.

## Historical anchors

**Corinth** and Paul's strained relationship with it
([[context.greco-roman-world]], [[context.roman-empire]]), where **honor, shame,
and boasting** ([[context.honor-shame]]) and **patronage and financial support**
([[context.patronage]]) — including the collection for Jerusalem — are central.
Pauline authorship is the scholarly consensus; the letter's composition and the
identity of the opponents are debated (see [[core.epistemic-humility]]).

## Literary features

Look for **self-defense (apology)** and the **"fool's speech,"** the
**strength-in-weakness** paradox, sharp **tonal shifts** between sections, the
**collection** appeal, **irony** and pointed rhetoric, the use of **Old Testament**
(e.g., the new-covenant and Moses material — see [[core.intertextuality]]), and
**Greek** wording bearing on meaning (see [[language.greek]]). Do not assume every
feature is present in a given passage; look for them.

## Key interpretive questions

Pose, do not answer:

- What is Paul defending or appealing for here, and against what pressure?
- How do honor, shame, and the conventions of boasting illuminate the passage?
- How does the strength-in-weakness theme shape the argument?
- How does the section's tone (tender, ironic, severe) bear on its reading?
- How does Paul use earlier Scripture in this passage?
- What does a debated term or construction mean in context, and what are the options?

## Common misreadings

- Reading the letter without attention to the conflict and the rival teachers behind it.
- Mistaking the ironic "fool's speech" boasting for straightforward self-promotion.
- Flattening the letter's sharp tonal shifts or ignoring the composition question.
- Lifting verses out of the defense-of-ministry argument they serve.

## Handling uncertainty

The letter's composition (whether it joins several letters), the identity of the
opponents, and the situation behind specific passages are debated. Present the
major responsible interpretations and their basis, label confidence
(see [[core.epistemic-humility]]), and leave the conclusion to the reader.
