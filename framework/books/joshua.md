## Purpose

This module helps you approach Joshua as the narrative of entering and
distributing the land — covenant promise meeting conquest, with all the
interpretive and ethical questions that raises. It is a hermeneutic profile, not
a commentary: it tells you what to notice and ask, assuming Core, Genre, and
Context are already loaded. It does not tell you what Joshua means.

## When to apply

When interpreting any passage in Joshua, on top of [[genre.narrative]] and the
ancient Near Eastern background in [[context.ancient-near-east]].

## Genre signals

Predominantly **narrative** ([[genre.narrative]]) — campaign accounts and
**land-distribution** material — interwoven with **lists** (boundaries, towns,
allotments), embedded **speeches** and covenant-renewal scenes, and occasional
**etiological notes** ("to this day"). Read the geographical lists by their own
conventions rather than as narrative prose.

## Historical anchors

The **ancient Near East** ([[context.ancient-near-east]]) — ANE warfare
conventions and the language of total destruction (*ḥerem*), settlement, and land
tenure — and the **covenant** promise of land and its obligations
([[context.covenant]]). The history and archaeology of the settlement are heavily
debated; hold reconstructions with appropriate confidence
(see [[core.epistemic-humility]]).

## Literary features

Look for **repeated motifs** (faithfulness, the land, "be strong and courageous"),
**type-scenes** (spies, crossings echoing the exodus), **geographical and
boundary lists**, **covenant-renewal** structure, and the strong **reuse of
Deuteronomy and the Pentateuch** that runs through the book
(see [[core.intertextuality]]). Do not assume every feature is present in a given
passage; look for them.

## Key interpretive questions

Pose, do not answer:

- How does this episode relate the promise of land to its fulfillment or its conditions?
- Is the passage reporting events, recording allotments, or commending a response?
- What do the warfare and *ḥerem* texts mean in their ancient setting, and what major approaches exist to their ethical and theological difficulty?
- What would Joshua's ancient audience have assumed that a modern reader might miss?
- How does the book reuse the exodus and Deuteronomy, and to what effect?
- How does the narrator evaluate the characters and the nation here?

## Common misreadings

- Reading the conquest narratives without engaging the genuine interpretive and ethical questions they raise — or, conversely, settling those questions by assumption.
- Treating narrated actions as automatically endorsed or as direct commands for today.
- Skipping the boundary and allotment lists as filler rather than asking their function.
- Resolving debated historical and archaeological questions by assertion rather than observation.

## Handling uncertainty

The history of the settlement, the relation to archaeology, the function of the
*ḥerem* language, and the book's composition are debated and tradition-linked, and
the ethical questions are read very differently across traditions. Present the
major approaches, separate observation from reconstruction, and label confidence
(see [[core.epistemic-humility]]); leave conclusions to the reader.
