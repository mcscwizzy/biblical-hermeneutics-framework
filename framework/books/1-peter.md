## Purpose

This module helps you approach 1 Peter as a letter to scattered believers under
social pressure — framing them as exiles and a chosen people, and shaping how they
live and suffer with hope. It is a hermeneutic profile, not a commentary: it tells
you what to notice and ask, assuming Core, Genre, and Context are already loaded. It
does not tell you what 1 Peter means.

## When to apply

When interpreting any passage in 1 Peter, on top of [[genre.epistle]] and the
Greco-Roman social world of pressure and status ([[context.greco-roman-world]],
[[context.roman-empire]], [[context.honor-shame]]).

## Genre signals

An **epistle** ([[genre.epistle]]) addressed to believers across several provinces,
combining **encouragement under suffering**, **paraenesis** (ethical exhortation)
including a **household code**, and possible **liturgical or baptismal** language.
Read the exhortation and the suffering material according to their aims.

## Historical anchors

The **Greco-Roman** world ([[context.greco-roman-world]], [[context.roman-empire]])
where these believers were a social minority facing suspicion and hostility, and the
**honor-shame** dynamics ([[context.honor-shame]]) behind the household code and the
counsel on conduct before outsiders. Authorship and the nature of the "suffering"
(local hostility vs. official action) are debated (see [[core.epistemic-humility]]).

## Literary features

Look for the **exile/diaspora** and **chosen-people** imagery applied to the church,
the **suffering-and-hope** theme, the **household code**, the call to honorable
conduct **before outsiders**, possible **liturgical** fragments, the difficult
"**spirits in prison**" passage, and heavy reuse of **Old Testament** language and
images (see [[core.intertextuality]]); **Greek** wording can bear on meaning
(see [[language.greek]]). Do not assume every feature is present in a given passage;
look for them.

## Key interpretive questions

Pose, do not answer:

- What kind of pressure or suffering is this passage addressing, so far as it can be discerned?
- How do exile, election, and honor-shame imagery shape the passage?
- How does the household code reflect, and how might it qualify, its social setting?
- How does 1 Peter reuse Old Testament language and identity here?
- What major approaches exist to a difficult passage (e.g., the "spirits in prison"), and what are the trade-offs?

## Common misreadings

- Reading the household code or counsel on suffering apart from the minority social setting.
- Over-decoding the "spirits in prison" passage rather than weighing the major approaches.
- Treating the exile/election imagery as incidental rather than central to the letter's framing.
- Reading the suffering language as necessarily official persecution without weighing the evidence.

## Handling uncertainty

Authorship, the date, and the precise nature of the suffering, along with the
"spirits in prison" passage, are debated and read differently across traditions.
Present the major responsible interpretations and their basis, label confidence
(see [[core.epistemic-humility]]), and leave the conclusion to the reader.
