## Purpose

This module helps you approach Haggai as post-exilic prophecy with a single urgent
concern: rousing the returned community to rebuild the temple, with precisely dated
oracles of rebuke and encouragement. It is a hermeneutic profile, not a commentary:
it tells you what to notice and ask, assuming Core, Genre, and Context are already
loaded. It does not tell you what Haggai means.

## When to apply

When interpreting any passage in Haggai, on top of [[genre.prophecy]] and the
Persian-period restoration setting in [[context.exile-and-restoration]].

## Genre signals

Brief prophetic **oracles** ([[genre.prophecy]]), mostly in **prose** rather than
extended poetry, presented in a tight **dated narrative** frame with a clear sequence
of messages and responses. Note the precise date formulae that structure the book.

## Historical anchors

The **Persian-period restoration** ([[context.exile-and-restoration]]) — the
returned community, economic hardship, and the stalled rebuilding of the **temple**
([[context.temple]]) under Zerubbabel and Joshua the high priest. Hold the
historical details with appropriate confidence (see [[core.epistemic-humility]]).

## Literary features

Look for the **precise date formulae**, the **message-and-response** structure,
**rhetorical questions** ("consider your ways"), the contrast between the people's
**houses and the LORD's house**, the encouragement that the new temple's glory will
exceed the former, and reuse of earlier temple and covenant language
(see [[core.intertextuality]]). Do not assume every feature is present in a given
passage; look for them.

## Key interpretive questions

Pose, do not answer:

- What is this dated oracle calling the community to do, and why now?
- How does the passage relate the people's circumstances to their neglect of the temple?
- Is the unit rebuke, encouragement, or promise?
- What do the promises about Zerubbabel and the temple's glory mean in their setting, and how are they later read?
- What would the restoration audience have assumed about the temple that a modern reader might miss?

## Common misreadings

- Reducing the book to a generic giving or building campaign apart from its restoration setting.
- Reading the temple-glory and Zerubbabel promises without attention to their post-exilic context and later reception.
- Treating the dated oracles as undatable timeless sayings.
- Importing modern economic assumptions into the book's cause-and-effect language.

## Handling uncertainty

Aspects of the book's composition and the reference of its promises (e.g., about
Zerubbabel) are debated and read differently across traditions. Present the major
approaches, distinguish observation from reconstruction, and label confidence
(see [[core.epistemic-humility]]); leave conclusions to the reader.
