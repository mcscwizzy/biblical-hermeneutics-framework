## Purpose

This module helps you approach Amos as eighth-century prophecy against the northern
kingdom's prosperity and injustice — sharp oracles indicting empty worship and the
oppression of the poor, with the day of the LORD turned against the complacent. It
is a hermeneutic profile, not a commentary: it tells you what to notice and ask,
assuming Core, Genre, and Context are already loaded. It does not tell you what
Amos means.

## When to apply

When interpreting any passage in Amos, on top of [[genre.prophecy]] and its Hebrew
**poetry** ([[genre.poetry]]).

## Genre signals

Prophetic **poetry** ([[genre.poetry]], [[genre.prophecy]]) — **oracles against the
nations**, **woe** and **judgment** oracles, **vision** reports, a brief
**biographical narrative** (the confrontation at Bethel), and **hymnic** fragments.
Identify which form is operating in a given passage.

## Historical anchors

The **northern kingdom** in the prosperous eighth century before its fall
([[context.israelite-monarchy]]), where social and economic injustice met
confident religiosity, judged against the **covenant** standard
([[context.covenant]]). Hold the historical details with appropriate confidence
(see [[core.epistemic-humility]]).

## Literary features

Look for the **"for three transgressions… and for four"** oracle pattern, the
sweep of **oracles against the nations** that turns on Israel, **vision** sequences,
sharp **social-justice** indictment paired with critique of **empty worship**, the
**day of the LORD** reversed against the hearers, and **hymnic** doxologies. Do not
assume every feature is present in a given passage; look for them.

## Key interpretive questions

Pose, do not answer:

- What injustice or false security is this oracle indicting, and against whom?
- How does the oracle-against-the-nations sequence set up its turn on Israel?
- Is the passage announcing judgment or holding out a conditional hope?
- How does the critique of worship relate to the critique of injustice here?
- What would the eighth-century audience have assumed about prosperity, worship, or the day of the LORD that a modern reader might miss?

## Common misreadings

- Reducing Amos to a generic social-justice slogan apart from its covenant and worship context.
- Reading the day-of-the-LORD reversal without its original sting against the complacent.
- Flattening the poetry's patterns and visions into flat prose.
- Treating the oracles against the nations as the book's point rather than its setup.

## Handling uncertainty

The arrangement of the oracles, the extent of later editing, and some historical
details are debated. Present the range, distinguish observation from
reconstruction, and label confidence (see [[core.epistemic-humility]]); leave
conclusions to the reader.
