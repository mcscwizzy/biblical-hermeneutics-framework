## Purpose

This module helps you approach the Song of Songs as a collection of Hebrew love
poetry — sensuous, image-rich verse exchanged between lovers — and to weigh
honestly the major ways it has been read. It is a hermeneutic profile, not a
commentary: it tells you what to notice and ask, assuming Core, Genre, and Context
are already loaded. It does not tell you what the Song means.

## When to apply

When interpreting any passage in the Song of Songs, on top of [[genre.poetry]] and
the ancient Near Eastern background of love poetry in
[[context.ancient-near-east]].

## Genre signals

Lyric **love poetry** ([[genre.poetry]]) — largely first-person speech between a
woman and a man, with a possible chorus — rich in **metaphor**, **dialogue**, and
sensory imagery. It is not narrative; do not impose a continuous plot where the
text gives lyric exchange. Its traditional association with Solomon and wisdom
links it loosely to the wisdom books ([[context.wisdom-tradition]]).

## Historical anchors

The wider **ancient Near East** ([[context.ancient-near-east]]), which produced
comparable love and wedding poetry, supplies background for its imagery and
conventions (note both likeness and difference, without assuming borrowing). Date,
authorship, structure, and unity are debated; hold them with appropriate
confidence (see [[core.epistemic-humility]]).

## Literary features

Look for **dialogue** between the lovers (and a possible chorus), **dense
metaphor and simile** drawn from nature, landscape, and royal imagery, **refrains**
(e.g., the charge "do not awaken love"), **wasf**-style descriptive praise of the
beloved's body, **sensory** and garden imagery, and shifts of **voice** and scene.
Do not assume every feature is present in a given passage; look for them.

## Key interpretive questions

Pose, do not answer:

- Who is speaking, to whom, and what is the imagery evoking in its setting?
- Which images are figurative, and how do they work as poetry rather than report?
- Is this lyric exchange, or is a connected scene actually indicated?
- Among the major approaches — reading the Song as human love poetry, as an allegory of divine love, or as a dramatic sequence — which does the passage itself support, and what are the trade-offs?
- What would the ancient audience have assumed about love poetry and its conventions that a modern reader might miss?

## Common misreadings

- Assuming an allegorical key (or, conversely, ruling allegory out) before observing the poetry on its own terms — the interpretive approach should be argued, not presupposed.
- Forcing a continuous plot onto what reads as lyric exchange.
- Decoding every body-image into a hidden referent rather than reading it as poetic praise.
- Settling debated questions of structure, unity, and approach by assumption rather than observation.

## Handling uncertainty

The Song's structure and unity, its date and authorship, and above all the major
interpretive approaches are read very differently across traditions. Present the
major approaches neutrally, distinguish observation from reconstruction, and label
confidence (see [[core.epistemic-humility]]); leave the conclusion to the reader.
