## Purpose

This module helps you approach Ezra as the narrative of return and rebuilding — the
restoration of the temple and the reordering of community life around the Law
under Persian rule. It is a hermeneutic profile, not a commentary: it tells you
what to notice and ask, assuming Core, Genre, and Context are already loaded. It
does not tell you what Ezra means.

## When to apply

When interpreting any passage in Ezra, on top of [[genre.narrative]] and the
Persian-period restoration in [[context.exile-and-restoration]].

## Genre signals

A mix of **narrative** ([[genre.narrative]]), official **documents and lists**
(decrees, correspondence, registers of returnees), and first-person **memoir**.
Note that parts are written in **Aramaic** (see [[language.hebrew]]). Read
documents and lists by their own conventions, not as narrative prose.

## Historical anchors

The **Persian-period restoration** ([[context.exile-and-restoration]]) — return
from Babylon, imperial authorization, and the rebuilding of the **temple**
([[context.temple]]) — with renewed attention to the **covenant** and the Law
([[context.covenant]]). The chronology of Ezra (and its relation to Nehemiah) and
the underlying history are debated; hold them with appropriate confidence
(see [[core.epistemic-humility]]).

## Literary features

Look for embedded **official documents** and **registers/lists**, the shift
between **Hebrew and Aramaic**, **first-person memoir** sections, **prayers** of
confession, the theme of **continuity** with pre-exilic worship, and the role of
imperial **decree** in enabling return. Do not assume every feature is present in a
given passage; look for them.

## Key interpretive questions

Pose, do not answer:

- How does this passage advance the rebuilding of temple, community, and covenant life?
- What is the function of the embedded document, list, or decree here?
- How does life under Persian authorization shape the situation?
- Is the passage reporting events or commending a response?
- What is at stake in the community-boundary and intermarriage material, and what major approaches exist to reading it?
- What would the restoration audience have assumed that a modern reader might miss?

## Common misreadings

- Skipping the lists and documents as filler rather than asking their function.
- Reading the sensitive intermarriage and separation material through modern categories without engaging its setting and the range of interpretive approaches.
- Treating narrated actions as automatically endorsed or prescribed for today.
- Settling the debated chronology of Ezra and Nehemiah by assumption rather than observation.

## Handling uncertainty

The chronology and order of events, the relation to Nehemiah, the sources and
documents, and how to read the separation material are debated and, in places,
sensitive. Present the major approaches, distinguish observation from
reconstruction, and label confidence (see [[core.epistemic-humility]]); leave
conclusions to the reader.
