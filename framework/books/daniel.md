## Purpose

This module helps you approach Daniel as a two-part book — court tales of
faithfulness in exile, then symbolic visions of empires and the end — read amid
the contested questions its visions raise. It is a hermeneutic profile, not a
commentary: it tells you what to notice and ask, assuming Core, Genre, and Context
are already loaded. It does not tell you what Daniel means, and it does not
adjudicate between the major interpretive approaches.

## When to apply

When interpreting any passage in Daniel, on top of [[genre.apocalyptic]] (the
visions) and [[genre.narrative]] (the court tales).

## Genre signals

A **mixed** book of two halves. The first is **narrative** ([[genre.narrative]]) —
court tales of Daniel and his companions under foreign kings. The second is
**apocalyptic** ([[genre.apocalyptic]]) — symbolic dream-visions of beasts,
kingdoms, and the end. Note that parts are written in **Aramaic**
(see [[language.hebrew]]). Identify which mode governs a given passage.

## Historical anchors

The setting is the **Babylonian and Persian** courts of the **exile**
([[context.exile-and-restoration]]); the book also belongs to the world of
**Second Temple** Jewish apocalyptic ([[context.second-temple-judaism]]). The
book's date and composition are among the most debated in the canon and are
tradition-linked; hold them with appropriate confidence
(see [[core.epistemic-humility]]).

## Literary features

Look for the **Hebrew–Aramaic** division (which cuts across the narrative/vision
split), **court-tale** patterns (test, deliverance, vindication), **dream and
vision** reports with their **interpretations**, **symbolic beasts, numbers, and
periods**, the "**son of man**" and kingdom imagery, and the dense reuse of
earlier Scripture and influence on later apocalyptic (see [[core.intertextuality]]).
Do not assume every feature is present in a given passage; look for them.

## Key interpretive questions

Pose, do not answer:

- Is this passage court narrative or apocalyptic vision — and how does that shape its claim?
- What does the symbol, beast, number, or period evoke, and how does the text itself interpret it?
- What is the situation of faithful life under foreign rule that the tales address?
- What are the major approaches to the visions and their referents, and what are the trade-offs?
- How does the passage reuse earlier Scripture, and how is it taken up later?
- What would a Second Temple audience have understood that a modern reader might miss?

## Common misreadings

- Reading the visions as a coded timetable of specific modern events.
- Treating the court tales and the apocalyptic visions by one set of rules.
- Settling the contested date, composition, and historical questions by assumption rather than observation.
- Decoding individual symbols while missing the passage's overall message of God's rule over kingdoms.

## Handling uncertainty

Daniel's date and composition, the historical questions in the tales, the
referents of the visions, and the major interpretive approaches are read very
differently across traditions. Present the major approaches neutrally, distinguish
observation from reconstruction, and label confidence
(see [[core.epistemic-humility]]); leave conclusions to the reader.
