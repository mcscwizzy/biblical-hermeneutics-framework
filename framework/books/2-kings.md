## Purpose

This module helps you approach 2 Kings as the narrative that carries both kingdoms
to their fall — the end of the north, then Judah into Babylonian exile — kings
still measured against covenant fidelity. It is a hermeneutic profile, not a
commentary: it tells you what to notice and ask, assuming Core, Genre, and Context
are already loaded. It does not tell you what 2 Kings means.

## When to apply

When interpreting any passage in 2 Kings, on top of [[genre.narrative]], the world
of the monarchy ([[context.israelite-monarchy]]), and — toward the end — the
**exile** ([[context.exile-and-restoration]]).

## Genre signals

Predominantly **narrative** ([[genre.narrative]]) organized by **regnal accounts**
with evaluative formulae, interwoven with **prophetic** narrative (Elisha) and
the accounts of national collapse and deportation. Distinguish the formulaic
regnal frame from the extended story cycles.

## Historical anchors

The later **monarchy** ([[context.israelite-monarchy]]) — the fall of the northern
kingdom to Assyria, the southern kingdom's decline, and the **exile** of Judah to
Babylon ([[context.exile-and-restoration]]) — judged within the **covenant**
standard the narrative applies to each king ([[context.covenant]]). The
chronology and history are debated; hold them with appropriate confidence
(see [[core.epistemic-humility]]).

## Literary features

Look for the **regnal evaluation formulae**, the **synchronized** narration of the
two kingdoms until the north falls, **prophetic narrative cycles**, **reform**
accounts (e.g., Hezekiah, Josiah), recurring motifs (idolatry, the "high places,"
the fulfilled word of the LORD), and the framing of **judgment and exile**. Do not
assume every feature is present in a given passage; look for them.

## Key interpretive questions

Pose, do not answer:

- How does this passage evaluate the king or kingdom against the covenant standard?
- How does it move the narrative toward the fall of the kingdoms and the exile?
- Is the passage reporting events or commending a response?
- How does the prophetic word function in relation to the kings and to the coming judgment?
- What would the ancient audience have assumed about Assyria, Babylon, exile, or reform that a modern reader might miss?
- How do the regnal formulae shape the way the narrative judges?

## Common misreadings

- Reading the regnal accounts as neutral chronicle rather than as evaluated, selective narrative.
- Confusing the northern and southern kingdoms and the empires that conquered them.
- Treating narrated royal actions as automatically endorsed or prescribed.
- Settling debated chronology and history by assumption rather than observation.

## Handling uncertainty

The chronology of the kings, the sources behind the regnal accounts, and the
book's composition and relation to the exile are debated. Present the range,
distinguish observation from reconstruction, and label confidence
(see [[core.epistemic-humility]]); leave conclusions to the reader.
