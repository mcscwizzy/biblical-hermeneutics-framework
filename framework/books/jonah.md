## Purpose

This module helps you approach Jonah as a prophetic *narrative* — unlike the other
Minor Prophets, it is mostly a story about a prophet, told with sustained irony,
that turns on God's mercy toward an enemy city. It is a hermeneutic profile, not a
commentary: it tells you what to notice and ask, assuming Core, Genre, and Context
are already loaded. It does not tell you what Jonah means.

## When to apply

When interpreting any passage in Jonah, on top of [[genre.narrative]] — the book's
primary mode — rather than reading it as a collection of oracles like the other
Minor Prophets.

## Genre signals

Primarily **narrative** ([[genre.narrative]]): a tightly plotted story about a
reluctant prophet, carried by **irony** and reversal. It contains a single brief
**oracle** ([[genre.prophecy]]) and an embedded **psalm** of thanksgiving in poetry
([[genre.poetry]]). Read the story's craft closely; the humor and irony are part of
the meaning.

## Historical anchors

The narrative is set in the era of the **Assyrian** empire, with **Nineveh** as the
great enemy city ([[context.israelite-monarchy]] supplies the monarchic/Assyrian
backdrop). The book's genre and historical character are much debated; hold them
with appropriate confidence (see [[core.epistemic-humility]]).

## Literary features

Look for pervasive **irony** (the pagan sailors and Ninevites respond better than
the prophet), **hyperbole** and comic exaggeration, **structural symmetry** between
the two halves, the embedded **psalm**, repeated motifs (down/flight, "great"
things, appointed creatures and plant), and the **open-ended question** that closes
the book. Note its reuse of stock prophetic and creedal language
(see [[core.intertextuality]]). Do not assume every feature is present in a given
passage; look for them.

## Key interpretive questions

Pose, do not answer:

- How does the irony shape this scene, and what is it exposing in the prophet?
- Is the passage reporting events or commending (or critiquing) a character's stance?
- How does the embedded psalm function within the surrounding narrative?
- What is the effect of the book's closing question being left unanswered?
- What major approaches exist to the book's genre and historical character, and what are their trade-offs?
- What would the original audience have assumed about Nineveh and Assyria that a modern reader might miss?

## Common misreadings

- Reading Jonah as a collection of oracles like the other Minor Prophets, missing that it is narrative.
- Fixating on the great fish while missing the book's irony and its concern with mercy.
- Treating Jonah as a simple hero to imitate rather than a figure the narrative critiques.
- Settling the debated questions of genre and historicity by assumption rather than observation.

## Handling uncertainty

The book's genre, historical character, date, and purpose are read very
differently across traditions. Present the major approaches, distinguish
observation from reconstruction, and label confidence
(see [[core.epistemic-humility]]); leave conclusions to the reader.
