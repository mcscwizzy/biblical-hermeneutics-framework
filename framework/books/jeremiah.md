## Purpose

This module helps you approach Jeremiah as the prophetic record of Judah's last
years and fall — poetic oracles of judgment and hope interwoven with prose sermons
and a vivid account of the prophet's own life and suffering. It is a hermeneutic
profile, not a commentary: it tells you what to notice and ask, assuming Core,
Genre, and Context are already loaded. It does not tell you what Jeremiah means.

## When to apply

When interpreting any passage in Jeremiah, on top of [[genre.prophecy]] and the
Hebrew **poetry** much of it uses ([[genre.poetry]]).

## Genre signals

A mix of poetic **oracles** ([[genre.prophecy]], [[genre.poetry]]), **prose
sermons**, **biographical narrative** about the prophet, **symbolic acts**,
**laments** ("confessions" of Jeremiah), **letters**, and oracles **against the
nations**. The book is not in strict chronological order; identify the form and
likely setting of a given unit.

## Historical anchors

The final decades of the **Israelite monarchy** ([[context.israelite-monarchy]]) —
Judah's last kings, Babylonian pressure, and the politics of resistance or
submission — running into the **exile** ([[context.exile-and-restoration]]),
including the promise of a new covenant beyond it. Composition and the differing
ancient text forms are debated; hold them with appropriate confidence
(see [[core.epistemic-humility]]).

## Literary features

Look for **judgment and hope** oracles, the prophet's **laments/confessions**,
**symbolic acts** (the potter, the yoke), **prose sermons** with covenant
language, recurring motifs (the new covenant, the "foe from the north," uprooting
and planting), non-chronological **arrangement**, and the book's reuse of earlier
Scripture and its own later influence (see [[core.intertextuality]]). Do not assume
every feature is present in a given passage; look for them.

## Key interpretive questions

Pose, do not answer:

- What situation in Judah's final years does this passage address, so far as it can be discerned?
- Is the unit poetic oracle, prose sermon, narrative, or symbolic act — and how does that shape its claim?
- How does the prophet's own suffering and lament function in the book?
- Is the passage announcing judgment, promising restoration, or both — and to whom?
- How does the new-covenant material relate to the book's wider message?
- What would the original audience have understood that a modern reader might miss?

## Common misreadings

- Reading the book as a chronological biography rather than a deliberately arranged collection.
- Treating poetic oracles and prose sermons by one set of rules.
- Lifting "plans to prosper you" or similar lines out of their exile setting and address.
- Settling debated questions of composition and text form by assumption rather than observation.

## Handling uncertainty

The book's composition, the relationship between its differing ancient text forms,
and the dating of individual units are debated. Present the range, distinguish
observation from reconstruction, and label confidence
(see [[core.epistemic-humility]]); leave conclusions to the reader.
