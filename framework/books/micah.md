## Purpose

This module helps you approach Micah as eighth-century prophecy to Judah —
alternating oracles of judgment on injustice and corrupt leadership with promises
of restoration and a coming ruler. It is a hermeneutic profile, not a commentary:
it tells you what to notice and ask, assuming Core, Genre, and Context are already
loaded. It does not tell you what Micah means.

## When to apply

When interpreting any passage in Micah, on top of [[genre.prophecy]] and its Hebrew
**poetry** ([[genre.poetry]]).

## Genre signals

Prophetic **poetry** ([[genre.poetry]], [[genre.prophecy]]) alternating **judgment**
and **salvation** oracles, with a **covenant lawsuit** (*rîb*) form, **woe**
oracles, and a closing appeal. Identify whether a unit indicts, promises, or pleads.

## Historical anchors

**Judah** (and the threat to both kingdoms) in the eighth century under Assyrian
pressure ([[context.israelite-monarchy]]), with social injustice and corrupt
leaders judged against the **covenant** standard ([[context.covenant]]). Hold the
historical details with appropriate confidence (see [[core.epistemic-humility]]).

## Literary features

Look for the **alternation of judgment and hope**, the **covenant-lawsuit** scene
("what does the LORD require"), indictment of **leaders, prophets, and the wealthy**,
the **ruler-from-Bethlehem** oracle, and **wordplay** on place-names, with reuse of
shared prophetic traditions (note the overlap with Isaiah's "nations to the
mountain" oracle — see [[core.intertextuality]]). Do not assume every feature is
present in a given passage; look for them.

## Key interpretive questions

Pose, do not answer:

- Is this unit judgment, promise, or covenant lawsuit, and to whom is it addressed?
- What injustice or failure of leadership is being indicted?
- Which images are figurative, and what did they evoke for an eighth-century audience?
- How does the passage relate the present judgment to the promised restoration?
- How does Micah's material overlap with other prophets, and what approaches exist to that relationship?

## Common misreadings

- Reading the famous summary lines (e.g., "do justice, love mercy") apart from the oracle and covenant context that give them force.
- Treating the alternation of judgment and hope as disorder rather than design.
- Flattening the poetry and its wordplay into flat prose.
- Settling debated questions of arrangement and the ruler oracle's reference by assumption rather than observation.

## Handling uncertainty

The arrangement of the oracles, the extent of editing, and the reference of the
ruler oracle are debated and read differently across traditions. Present the major
approaches, distinguish observation from reconstruction, and label confidence
(see [[core.epistemic-humility]]); leave conclusions to the reader.
