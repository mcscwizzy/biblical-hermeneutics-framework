## Purpose

This module helps you read the Gospels as theological narratives commonly
classified as a form of ancient biography: selective, purposefully arranged
accounts that present Jesus and blend story with teaching. Reading them well
combines narrative sensitivity with attention to each author's distinct shaping.

## When to apply

When interpreting Matthew, Mark, Luke, or John, or passages within them.

## Interpretive moves

1. **Apply narrative reading** (plot, characterization, structure) and recognize
   the teaching forms embedded within — parables, sayings, and discourses.
2. **Read each Gospel on its own terms first.** Note an author's selection,
   arrangement, and emphases before harmonizing the accounts.
3. **Attend to the first-century setting** — Jewish and Greco-Roman
   (see [[context.roman-empire]]) — and the earlier Scripture the Gospels
   invoke (see [[core.intertextuality]]).
4. **Interpret parables for their central point** in context, and resist
   allegorizing every detail.
5. **Distinguish the situation within the story** from the author's purpose in
   telling it to later readers.
6. **Consult the Greek where it bears on meaning** (see [[language.greek]])
   without overstating what a word or tense proves.

## Common errors to avoid

- Collapsing the four Gospels into one harmony that erases each author's emphasis.
- Allegorizing the details of parables.
- Treating every narrated detail as a direct command for readers today.
- Ignoring the Old Testament and Second-Temple background the Gospels assume.

## Handling uncertainty

Questions of sources, arrangement, and how the accounts relate are debated.
Present the responsible options, separate observation from inference, and label
your confidence (see [[core.epistemic-humility]]).
