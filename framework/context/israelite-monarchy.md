## Purpose

This module supplies the world of the Israelite and Judahite monarchy — the rise
of kingship, the united and then divided kingdoms, the royal court, and the
prophets who addressed it. It helps you read texts that assume that political and
religious order without importing modern statecraft. It surveys the setting; it
does not settle what a passage teaches.

## When to apply

When interpreting texts set in or addressed to the monarchy: Samuel, Kings, and
Chronicles; royal and Zion psalms; and the pre-exilic prophets who spoke to kings
and nations.

## Interpretive moves

1. **Place the text on the political map.** Distinguish the united monarchy from
   the later divided kingdoms (Israel in the north, Judah in the south), and note
   which audience and era a passage addresses.
2. **Read kingship in its ancient frame.** Royal ideology, dynasty, and the
   king's role in worship and justice were shaped by the wider ancient Near East
   (see [[context.ancient-near-east]]); weigh both likeness and difference.
3. **Connect king, covenant, and temple.** Promises to the dynasty, the
   Jerusalem temple, and covenant obligations (see [[context.covenant]],
   [[context.temple]]) frame how these texts evaluate rulers.
4. **Hear the prophets in their political moment** — addressing specific kings,
   alliances, and crises — rather than as timeless oracles detached from events.
5. **Note differing presentations.** Samuel–Kings and Chronicles narrate the same
   era with different emphases; observe each account's shaping before harmonizing.

## Common errors to avoid

- Reading modern nationhood, democracy, or church–state categories into the
  ancient monarchy.
- Collapsing the northern and southern kingdoms, or confusing their kings and
  timelines.
- Treating royal or Zion language as detached from its political setting.
- Asserting specific dates, regnal details, or reconstructions beyond the
  evidence (see [[core.anti-hallucination]]).

## Handling uncertainty

Chronology, the relationship among the sources, and the history behind the
narratives are actively debated. Distinguish well-attested background from
contested reconstruction, present alternatives where scholars differ, and label
your confidence (see [[core.epistemic-humility]]).
