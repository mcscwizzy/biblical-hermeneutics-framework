## Purpose

This module supplies the broader Hellenistic cultural world the New Testament
moved in — its language, education, philosophy, rhetoric, religion, and social
forms. Where [[context.roman-empire]] foregrounds imperial power and structure,
this module foregrounds the shared Greek-speaking culture that shaped how ideas
were argued, communities formed, and gods were worshipped. It surveys the
setting; it does not settle what a passage teaches.

## When to apply

When interpreting New Testament texts whose meaning engages Greco-Roman culture:
philosophical terms or commonplaces, rhetorical argument, athletic or theatrical
imagery, popular morality, religious cults and temples, voluntary associations,
and the conventions of Greek letters and speech (especially Acts and the
Epistles).

## Interpretive moves

1. **Hear cultural vocabulary.** Terms drawn from philosophy, athletics, the law
   court, or the marketplace carried recognized associations; weigh whether a
   given use engages that background.
2. **Recognize rhetorical conventions.** Greco-Roman speech and letter-writing
   had standard forms of persuasion; note how a text argues, not only what it
   says.
3. **Account for the religious landscape** — many gods, temples, mystery cults,
   and the imperial cult — as the plausibility structure an audience assumed.
4. **Note social forms.** Households, patronage, honor and shame
   (see [[context.honor-shame]]), and voluntary associations shaped how early
   communities were organized and perceived.
5. **Weigh engagement, not just parallel.** A shared term or form may be adopted,
   adapted, or contested; observe how the text uses it rather than assuming
   borrowing.

## Common errors to avoid

- Treating any parallel with a philosophy or cult as proof of dependence
  ("parallelomania").
- Flattening the diverse religious and philosophical scene into one "pagan"
  backdrop.
- Importing modern individualism, secularism, or categories of "religion" into
  the ancient setting.
- Asserting specific cultural facts or influences beyond the evidence
  (see [[core.anti-hallucination]]).

## Handling uncertainty

How strongly a text engages a philosophical school, cult, or rhetorical
convention is often debated. Distinguish well-attested background from contested
reconstruction, present alternatives where scholars differ, and label your
confidence (see [[core.epistemic-humility]]).
