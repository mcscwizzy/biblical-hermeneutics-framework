## Purpose

This module supplies the first-century Roman backdrop against which the New
Testament was written. It helps you read the political, social, and economic
realities a first-century audience took for granted — empire, citizenship,
slavery, and imperial ideology — without importing modern assumptions.

## When to apply

When interpreting New Testament texts whose meaning depends on the Roman world:
references to rulers, citizenship, taxation, slavery, courts, travel, military
imagery, or the language of "lord," "savior," and "gospel."

## Interpretive moves

1. **Locate the text in the imperial order.** Consider how Roman power,
   provincial administration, and local rulers shaped daily life for the
   audience.
2. **Hear imperial vocabulary.** Terms such as *gospel* (good news), *lord*,
   *savior*, and *peace* also carried public, imperial meanings; weigh whether a
   given use engages that background.
3. **Account for social structure.** Slavery, the household, citizenship, and
   sharp status differences were pervasive and shape many passages.
4. **Note the mixed setting** of many early communities — Jews and Gentiles,
   citizens and non-citizens, free and enslaved — and how that affects the text.
5. **Connect economy and travel** — roads, trade, patronage, and mobility — to
   the spread of letters and the situations they address.

## Common errors to avoid

- Reading modern nation-state politics or economics into the Roman setting.
- Assuming every use of "lord" or "gospel" is a deliberate anti-imperial
  statement — or that none is; weigh each case.
- Flattening first-century slavery into modern categories without noting the
  differences and the debate.
- Asserting specific historical details that the evidence does not support
  (see [[core.anti-hallucination]]).

## Handling uncertainty

Much about local conditions and how strongly a text engages imperial ideology is
debated. Distinguish well-established background (consensus) from contested
readings, and label your confidence (see [[core.epistemic-humility]]).
