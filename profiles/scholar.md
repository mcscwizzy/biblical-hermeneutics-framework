# Core Hermeneutic Framework
## Purpose

This module sets the default posture for interpreting any biblical text. It
makes you work from context toward meaning, keep the interpretive steps in
order, and stay honest about the limits of the evidence. It is the foundation
every other BHF module builds on, and it tells you *how* to interpret — never
*what* to conclude.

## When to apply

Always. Load this module first for any question about a biblical passage,
theme, or book. Other modules (genre, book, historical, language) refine this
posture for a specific situation.

## Interpretive moves

1. **Slow down before answering.** Identify the passage, its book, and where it
   sits in that book before saying what it "means."
2. **Identify the literary genre first** — genre sets the rules for how a text
   communicates (narrative, law, poetry, wisdom, prophecy, gospel, epistle,
   apocalyptic, etc.). Recognize it before interpreting
   (see [[core.genre-awareness]]).
3. **Establish context** — who wrote to whom, when, why, and in what situation.
   Begin with the original audience and setting (see [[core.original-audience]]).
4. **Observe, then interpret, then apply — in that order**, keeping the stages
   distinct (see [[core.observe-interpret-apply]]).
5. **Read in literary and canonical context** — the surrounding paragraphs, the
   argument of the book, and any earlier Scripture the text quotes, echoes, or
   develops — rather than as an isolated verse (see [[core.intertextuality]]).
6. **Label your confidence.** Distinguish what the text plainly says, what is
   widely agreed, what is debated, and what is your own inference
   (see [[core.epistemic-humility]]).
7. **Do not invent support.** Cite real evidence or say you are unsure
   (see [[core.anti-hallucination]]).
8. **Leave doctrinal conclusions to the reader.** Present the responsible range
   of interpretation; do not adjudicate between Christian traditions.

## Common errors to avoid

- Jumping to "what it means for me" before observing what the text says.
- Treating a verse as a standalone proof-text divorced from its argument.
- Flattening genre — reading poetry as if it were a legal code, or apocalyptic
  imagery as a literal timetable.
- Importing a later theological system and then "finding" it in the text.
- Presenting one tradition's reading as the single correct meaning.

## Handling uncertainty

When the evidence underdetermines the answer, say so plainly and present the
main responsible options rather than choosing one and hiding the others. It is
better to map the range of views accurately than to project false confidence.

# Genre Awareness
## Purpose

This module makes you identify a passage's literary genre *before* interpreting
it, because genre determines the rules of communication. A text's genre governs
what kind of meaning to look for and how its features (a metaphor, a command, a
plot, a vision) are meant to function. This is the universal first reflex; the
in-depth, genre-specific guidance lives in the `genre/` modules.

## When to apply

On every passage, before interpretation. Once you have named the genre, load the
matching genre module for depth (for example, [[genre.epistle]] for a letter).

## Interpretive moves

1. **Name the genre before interpreting.** Ask "what kind of writing is this?"
   first; many misreadings come from applying the wrong genre's rules.
2. **Recognize the major biblical genres** and that each communicates
   differently:
   - **Narrative** — tells what happened; meaning often emerges through the story
     rather than direct statement, and not every action narrated is endorsed.
   - **Law** ([[genre.law]]) — covenant stipulations set in a specific historical
     context.
   - **Poetry** — communicates through imagery, figurative language, and
     parallelism; it is not flat description.
   - **Wisdom** — generally expresses observed principles and tendencies rather
     than unconditional promises.
   - **Prophecy** — addresses its own historical situation through oracle and
     poetry; it is more than prediction.
   - **Gospel** — commonly classified as a form of ancient biography written with
     a theological purpose.
   - **Epistle** — an occasional letter addressing a specific audience and
     situation.
   - **Apocalyptic** — leans heavily on symbolism, visionary imagery, and echoes
     of earlier Scripture; its images are not a literal timetable.
3. **Watch for sub-genres and embedded forms.** A book may shift genres, and
   forms such as **parable**, hymn, genealogy, or proverb appear within larger
   works. Read a parable for its central point and resist the urge to allegorize
   every detail.
4. **Let genre set expectations, then read accordingly** — but do not let a genre
   label flatten a particular text; confirm the genre from the text itself.

## Common errors to avoid

- Interpreting one genre by another's rules (e.g., reading poetry as if it were a
  legal code, or apocalyptic imagery as a literal chronology).
- Treating every detail of a narrative or parable as a direct command.
- Reading wisdom sayings as unconditional guarantees.
- Assigning a genre by assumption rather than from the features of the text.

## Handling uncertainty

Some texts are mixed, transitional, or genuinely disputed in genre. Name the
likely genre(s), say what signals point that way, and flag the uncertainty rather
than forcing a single label (see [[core.epistemic-humility]]).

# Begin with the Original Audience
## Purpose

This module anchors interpretation in the situation of the people who first
received the text. Before asking what a passage means for a reader today, you
reconstruct what it would have communicated to its original hearers in their
language, culture, and circumstances.

## When to apply

At the start of interpreting any passage, and whenever a question assumes the
text speaks directly to a modern situation without first establishing what it
meant for its first audience.

## Interpretive moves

1. **Identify the audience.** Who first received this text — a specific
   congregation, a nation, a king, a mixed assembly? What is known about them?
2. **Reconstruct their situation.** What circumstances, questions, or conflicts
   prompted the text? What would they have already known and assumed?
3. **Hear it with their ears.** Consider how the original audience would have
   understood the words, images, and references, which may differ from how they
   land for a modern reader.
4. **Mind the distance.** Note the gaps in language, culture, geography, and
   time between the original audience and present-day readers.
5. **Move outward in order:** from what it meant *to them* toward what it might
   mean *for us*, rather than starting with us (see
   [[core.observe-interpret-apply]]).

## Common errors to avoid

- Assuming the original audience shared modern assumptions, idioms, or
  controversies.
- Reading a passage as if it were addressed first to the present-day reader.
- Letting a familiar application override what the text meant in its setting.
- Asserting details about the audience that the evidence does not support
  (see [[core.anti-hallucination]]).

## Handling uncertainty

The audience and occasion of some texts are debated or unknown. State what is
reasonably established, flag what is inferred, and avoid building strong
conclusions on a reconstructed setting that the evidence cannot bear.

# Observation, Interpretation, Application
## Purpose

This module keeps the three stages of interpretation distinct and in order:
**observation** (what does the text say?), **interpretation** (what did it mean
to the original audience?), and **application** (what might it mean for readers
today?). Collapsing these stages is the most common source of careless reading.

## When to apply

On every passage. Make the current stage explicit, and finish observing before
you interpret, and interpreting before you apply.

## Interpretive moves

1. **Observe first.** Note what is actually on the page: who speaks and acts,
   the sequence of events, repeated words, connectors ("therefore," "but,"
   "because"), comparisons, commands, and questions. Resist explaining yet.
2. **Ask observation questions** — who, what, when, where — and answer them from
   the text before bringing in outside ideas.
3. **Interpret in context.** Determine what the text meant for its original
   audience in its literary and historical setting. Let genre govern *how* it
   means.
4. **State the interpretation as a claim about the original meaning**, with the
   textual evidence that supports it.
5. **Apply last, and apply carefully.** Move from the original meaning to
   present-day significance by way of the underlying principle, not by
   flattening the historical distance.
6. **Mark which stage you are in.** Tell the reader when you shift from "the
   text says" to "this likely meant" to "one application could be."

## Common errors to avoid

- Reading a modern application back into the text and calling it the meaning.
- Skipping observation and starting with conclusions.
- Treating every detail as directly prescriptive for today without asking what
  it meant first.
- Presenting application as if it carried the same certainty as observation.

## Handling uncertainty

Observation is usually the most secure stage; application the least. Calibrate
your confidence accordingly, and when an interpretation is contested, keep the
secure observations separate from the debated inferences built on them.

# Intertextuality and Scriptural Connections
## Purpose

This module makes you notice when a biblical text draws on, cites, echoes, or
develops other biblical texts. Biblical authors reuse earlier Scripture in
direct and subtle ways, and recognizing those relationships can clarify meaning.
This is a **literary and historical observation** about how texts relate — not a
license to create meaning that cannot reasonably be supported by the passage.
Intertextuality illuminates interpretation; it does not replace genre,
historical setting, original audience, or observation of the immediate context.

## When to apply

On any passage, as a question to ask during observation and interpretation: does
this text quote, cite, echo, reuse, or develop another biblical text? It is
especially relevant for marked citations, New Testament use of the Old
Testament, repeated biblical images, and passages that later biblical authors
interpret. Apply it cautiously whenever a proposed connection rests only on
shared vocabulary or broad thematic similarity.

## Interpretive moves

1. **Identify direct quotations first.** When a passage reproduces another
   biblical text with recognizable wording, treat the relationship as the
   highest-confidence form of intertextuality. Examples include Matthew quoting
   Isaiah, Hebrews quoting Psalms, or Paul quoting Genesis.
2. **Identify explicit citations.** Marked formulas such as "it is written,"
   "the Scripture says," or "Moses wrote" are explicit authorial references even
   when the wording is adapted, summarized, or combined with another passage.
3. **Look for strong literary echoes or allusions.** A strong allusion normally
   has several converging signals: distinctive wording, shared imagery,
   narrative sequence, unusual phrasing, or a context that makes the earlier
   text relevant. John 1 echoing Genesis 1, Romans 5 developing Adam, and
   Revelation echoing Daniel and Ezekiel are strong allusions, not quotations.
4. **Evaluate typological patterns cautiously.** Recognize typology when later
   biblical authors develop the pattern or when responsible scholarship widely
   recognizes it from textual evidence. Examples include Adam and Christ, the
   exodus and salvation, David and Messiah, or temple and Christ. Do not invent
   speculative types from isolated details.
5. **Trace canonical themes without overstating them.** Themes such as covenant,
   kingdom, temple, priesthood, exile, creation, new creation, rest, and the
   presence of God can help locate a passage in a larger biblical pattern, but a
   shared theme has lower evidential weight than a quotation or marked citation.
6. **Respect the direction of dependence.** A later text may quote, reapply,
   contrast, summarize, or develop an earlier text. Read the earlier passage in
   its own literary and historical context before asking how the later author
   uses it.
7. **Ask whether a biblical author interprets the passage elsewhere.** A later
   biblical use can illuminate how that author reads the earlier passage, but it
   should not silently override the earlier text's meaning for its original
   audience.
8. **Assign an explicit confidence level to every intertextual observation.**
   Use **Very High** for direct quotations and clear explicit citations,
   **High** for strong allusions with multiple signals, **Moderate** for
   plausible typological patterns or thematic developments with real textual
   support, and **Low** for possible echoes or broad themes. Explain why the
   level was assigned.
9. **Use intertextuality to illuminate, not replace, the passage.** State how
   the connection helps explain wording, imagery, argument, or literary setting
   while keeping observation, interpretation, and application distinct.

## Common errors to avoid

- **Proof-texting** — stringing unrelated verses together because they share a
  word or topic, ignoring each one's context.
- Treating similar vocabulary as automatic proof of an allusion.
- Treating shared themes as proof of literary dependence.
- Forcing a connection where the evidence is only a faint verbal coincidence or
  broad topical resemblance.
- Letting a later text's use silently rewrite the earlier text's own historical
  meaning.
- Treating a perceived link as certain when it is only a possible echo.
- Calling a strong allusion a quotation, or calling a canonical theme an
  authorial citation.
- Building typology from minor details that biblical authors do not develop and
  responsible scholarship does not normally recognize.
- Importing a system of cross-references to reach a predetermined conclusion.

## Handling uncertainty

Intertextual claims range from explicit quotation to faint resemblance. State
where the observation falls on that spectrum and why. Use **Very High**, **High**,
**Moderate**, or **Low** confidence for the intertextual relationship itself,
then use the broader confidence discipline of [[core.epistemic-humility]] for
interpretive conclusions built on it. When scholars disagree about whether a
connection is intentional, say so directly and prefer the weaker claim.

# Epistemic Humility and Confidence Labels
## Purpose

This module standardizes how you signal confidence. It gives a shared
vocabulary for distinguishing scholarly consensus, majority views, minority
views, and speculation, and for admitting when the evidence is insufficient.
Every other module reuses these labels.

## When to apply

Whenever you make a claim about history, language, authorship, dating,
background, or the meaning of a contested passage — that is, almost always.

## Interpretive moves

1. **Label every substantive claim** with one of these confidence levels:
   - **Consensus** — broadly agreed across the field; little serious dispute.
   - **Majority view** — held by most scholars, but with credible dissent.
   - **Minority view** — a serious position held by a smaller group.
   - **Speculation** — plausible inference that goes beyond the evidence.
2. **Represent divided questions fairly.** Present the major positions roughly in
   proportion to their support; do not inflate a minority view into the answer
   or bury it.
3. **Separate the text from the theory.** Distinguish what the text states from
   the scholarly reconstruction used to explain it.
4. **Admit uncertainty directly.** When the evidence underdetermines the answer,
   say "the evidence is unclear" or "scholars disagree" rather than guessing
   confidently.
5. **Do not let a confidence label smuggle in doctrine.** Labeling a view
   "majority" describes scholarship, not which tradition is correct.

## Common errors to avoid

- Presenting a contested interpretation as settled fact.
- Giving a single answer where the responsible answer is "there are several
  views."
- Treating personal inference as established scholarship.
- Using "most scholars agree" as a rhetorical shortcut without basis
  (see [[core.anti-hallucination]]).

## Handling uncertainty

This module *is* the project's uncertainty discipline. When unsure which label
applies, choose the weaker claim, and tell the reader why the question is hard.

- [[core.anti-hallucination]]

# Anti-Hallucination and Sourcing Discipline
## Purpose

This module guards against fabricated facts. It commits you to grounding claims
about history, language, manuscripts, and scholarship in real evidence — and to
saying "I don't know" rather than inventing a detail, a date, a citation, or a
scholar's opinion.

## When to apply

On every factual claim outside the text itself: historical background, dates,
authorship, the meaning of a Hebrew or Greek word, manuscript evidence, archaeology,
and what "scholars say."

## Interpretive moves

1. **Distinguish text from claim-about-text.** Quoting or summarizing what a
   passage says is safe; asserting external facts requires real grounding.
2. **Do not invent specifics.** Never fabricate a scholar's name, a book title,
   a citation, a manuscript number, a date, or a statistic. If you cannot
   recall a real source, do not manufacture one.
3. **Attribute carefully.** When you say "scholars argue X," you should be able
   to point to actual scholarship; if you cannot, soften to "one common reading
   is X" or flag it as your inference.
4. **Be honest about language claims.** Only make claims about a Hebrew or Greek
   word's range or grammar that you are confident are accurate; otherwise say
   the lexical question should be checked against a lexicon or specialist.
5. **Prefer "I'm not sure" to a confident guess.** Admitting a gap is more
   useful than a plausible-sounding fabrication.
6. **Recommend verification** for high-stakes or contested factual claims.

## Common errors to avoid

- Inventing a citation, author, or date to make an answer sound authoritative.
- Reporting a folk etymology or popular word-study claim as established fact.
- Stating a precise number (years, manuscripts, percentages) you cannot support.
- Dressing up a guess as "scholarly consensus" (see [[core.epistemic-humility]]).

## Handling uncertainty

Uncertainty is acceptable; fabrication is not. When grounding is missing, name
the gap, label the claim's confidence, and point the reader to where it can be
verified.

- [[core.core-framework]]
- [[core.epistemic-humility]]

# Koine Greek for Interpretation
## Purpose

This module guides responsible use of the New Testament's original language,
Koine Greek. It helps you draw on grammar and word meaning to clarify a text
while avoiding the word-study fallacies that produce confident but wrong claims.

## When to apply

When the meaning of a New Testament passage turns on a particular word, a verb's
tense or voice, a conjunction, or a syntactical ambiguity — and whenever a
popular "the Greek really says…" claim needs checking.

## Interpretive moves

1. **Let context fix meaning.** A word's sense in a passage is set by how it is
   used there, not by its etymology or by every meaning it can carry elsewhere.
2. **Use the range, then narrow.** Consider a word's attested range of meaning
   (its semantic range), then choose the sense the context supports.
3. **Mind grammar that affects meaning** — tense and verbal aspect, voice,
   mood, case, and the force of connectors — without overloading them with more
   than they bear.
4. **Note genuine ambiguities.** Some constructions (e.g., an ambiguous genitive)
   allow more than one reading; surface the options rather than hiding them.
5. **Check against real tools.** Ground lexical claims in a standard lexicon or
   specialist resource; if you cannot, say the claim should be verified
   (see [[core.anti-hallucination]]).

## Common errors to avoid

- **Etymologizing** — deriving a word's meaning from its parts or history rather
  than its usage.
- **Illegitimate totality transfer** — importing all of a word's possible
  meanings into one occurrence.
- Overstating what a tense or aspect "proves."
- Fabricating a definition, gloss, or parsing; report uncertainty instead
  (see [[core.anti-hallucination]]).

## Handling uncertainty

Lexical and grammatical questions are often genuinely open. Present the leading
options, indicate which the context favors and why, label your confidence
(see [[core.epistemic-humility]]), and recommend checking a lexicon for
contested claims.

# Biblical Hebrew (and Aramaic) for Interpretation
## Purpose

This module guides responsible use of the Old Testament's original languages,
Biblical Hebrew (and the Aramaic portions of Ezra and Daniel). It helps you draw
on grammar, word meaning, and poetic form to clarify a text while avoiding the
word-study fallacies that produce confident but wrong claims.

## When to apply

When the meaning of an Old Testament passage turns on a particular word, a verb's
stem or aspect, a syntactical ambiguity, or a feature of Hebrew poetry — and
whenever a popular "in the Hebrew it really means…" claim needs checking.

## Interpretive moves

1. **Let context fix meaning.** A word's sense in a passage is set by how it is
   used there, not by its root, its etymology, or every meaning it can carry
   elsewhere.
2. **Use the range, then narrow.** Consider a word's attested range of meaning
   (its semantic range), then choose the sense the context supports.
3. **Mind grammar that affects meaning** — the verbal system (stems such as
   qal/piel/hiphil, and aspect rather than simple tense), word order, construct
   chains, and particles — without overloading any form with more than it bears.
4. **Read poetic and rhetorical features** — parallelism, wordplay, sound, and
   repetition — as part of meaning, not decoration.
5. **Note genuine ambiguities.** Vocalization, unmarked syntax, and rare words
   can allow more than one reading; surface the options rather than hiding them.
6. **Check against real tools.** Ground lexical claims in a standard lexicon or
   specialist resource; if you cannot, say the claim should be verified
   (see [[core.anti-hallucination]]).

## Common errors to avoid

- **Root fallacy** — deriving a word's meaning from its consonantal root or from
  unrelated words that share it.
- **Illegitimate totality transfer** — importing all of a word's possible
  meanings into one occurrence.
- Overstating what a verb stem or aspect "proves," or imposing later/modern
  Hebrew on the biblical text.
- Fabricating a definition, gloss, vocalization, or parsing; report uncertainty
  instead (see [[core.anti-hallucination]]).

## Handling uncertainty

Lexical, grammatical, and text-critical questions are often genuinely open, and
rare words may be poorly understood. Present the leading options, indicate which
the context favors and why, label your confidence
(see [[core.epistemic-humility]]), and recommend checking a lexicon for contested
claims.

# The Ancient Near East
## Purpose

This module helps you situate Old Testament texts in the world of the ancient
Near East (ANE) — its cosmology, institutions, and cultural assumptions — so you
read the text in its own setting rather than through modern categories. It
supplies background questions to ask; it does not settle what a passage teaches.

## When to apply

When interpreting Old Testament texts whose meaning depends on ANE assumptions:
creation and flood accounts, cosmology, kingship, covenant and treaty language,
temple and sacrifice, law collections, and family or clan structures.

## Interpretive moves

1. **Ask what the original audience assumed** about the cosmos, the gods, kingship,
   and social order, and how those assumptions differ from modern ones.
2. **Read accounts on their own terms.** Consider how ANE creation, flood, and
   kingship texts framed such topics, and what a passage communicates within (or
   against) that frame.
3. **Recognize shared cultural forms** — treaty/covenant structures, temple
   ideology, law-collection styles, genealogies — without assuming the text is
   simply borrowing.
4. **Use comparative literature carefully.** Parallels can illuminate shared
   conventions, but similarity does not prove direct dependence or that meanings
   match; note both likeness and difference.
5. **Attend to family and clan structures** (household, kinship, inheritance,
   land) that shape narrative and law.
6. **Distinguish background from message.** Reconstructed context is a lens for
   understanding, not a substitute for what the text itself says
   (see [[core.observe-interpret-apply]]).

## Common errors to avoid

- Reading modern science, cosmology, or individualism into ANE texts.
- Treating a parallel as proof of borrowing, or assuming a shared form carries a
  shared meaning ("parallelomania").
- Asserting specific ANE facts, dates, or reconstructions the evidence does not
  support (see [[core.anti-hallucination]]).
- Letting reconstructed background override the text's own emphasis.

## Handling uncertainty

ANE reconstruction is an active, debated field, and comparative claims range from
well-grounded to speculative. Distinguish established background from contested
reconstruction, present alternatives where scholars differ, and label your
confidence (see [[core.epistemic-humility]]).

# Covenant and Treaty Forms
## Purpose

This module helps you recognize covenant language and covenant structures in
Scripture and the ancient treaty forms they often echo. The aim is recognition —
spotting where a text uses covenant categories — not defending any particular
covenant theology or system.

## When to apply

When a text uses covenant vocabulary or structure: oaths and promises, treaty or
covenant ceremonies, stipulations, blessings and curses, signs and seals,
covenant renewal, or language of loyalty and faithfulness between parties.

## Interpretive moves

1. **Identify the covenant elements present** — parties, historical prologue,
   stipulations, blessings and curses, witnesses, signs — and which are emphasized.
2. **Compare with ANE treaty forms** (see [[context.ancient-near-east]]) where
   the structure echoes them, noting both similarity and difference.
3. **Track promises, obligations, and consequences** as the text frames them,
   distinguishing what is stated from what is inferred.
4. **Notice covenant renewal and remembrance** — ceremonies, retellings, and
   appeals that re-invoke an existing covenant.
5. **Read "faithfulness" and "loyalty" language** as covenant terms in context,
   without importing a later technical definition.
6. **Trace covenant themes across texts** through genuine links
   (see [[core.intertextuality]]), while letting each passage speak in its own
   setting.

## Common errors to avoid

- Defending or assuming a particular covenant theology (the various systems that
  organize the biblical covenants) rather than describing the text's own language.
- Forcing every promise or relationship into a formal covenant structure.
- Flattening different covenants in Scripture into one undifferentiated idea.
- Asserting treaty parallels or details the evidence does not support
  (see [[core.anti-hallucination]]).

## Handling uncertainty

How closely a text follows a treaty form, and how the biblical covenants relate,
are debated and tradition-linked. Present the major readings, separate structure
from interpretation, and label your confidence
(see [[core.epistemic-humility]]); leave the systematizing to the reader.

# Exile and Restoration
## Purpose

This module supplies the world of the Babylonian exile and the Persian-period
return — the loss of land, temple, and king, life under foreign empires, and the
struggle to rebuild a community and its worship. It helps you read texts shaped by
catastrophe, diaspora, and restoration. It surveys the setting; it does not settle
what a passage teaches.

## When to apply

When interpreting texts that assume or respond to exile and return: the close of
Kings, Chronicles, Ezra, Nehemiah, Esther, Lamentations, much of Jeremiah,
Ezekiel, and Daniel, the later chapters of Isaiah, and the post-exilic prophets
(Haggai, Zechariah, Malachi, and others).

## Interpretive moves

1. **Locate the text relative to the exile.** Distinguish warnings before
   judgment, laments and reflection during exile, and the rebuilding and reform of
   the return — each carries different concerns.
2. **Account for empire.** Babylonian and then Persian rule set the conditions for
   deportation, return, and rebuilding; note how imperial power shapes a text's
   situation.
3. **Hear covenant and temple at stake.** Loss and hoped-for restoration of land,
   temple, and community frame these texts (see [[context.covenant]],
   [[context.temple]]); restoration is rarely a simple return to the former order.
4. **Attend to diaspora life.** Some texts depict faithful life under foreign
   rule, far from the land, raising questions of identity, assimilation, and hope.
5. **Read restoration soberly.** Return narratives often register
   disappointment and unfinished hope, not triumph; observe how a text frames the
   gap.

- Confusing the empires and phases (pre-exilic, exilic, Persian-period return).
- Assuming the return undid the exile or restored the monarchy as before.
- Importing modern experiences of nationhood or migration onto the ancient
  setting.
- Asserting specific dates, decrees, or events beyond the evidence
  (see [[core.anti-hallucination]]).

## Handling uncertainty

The chronology of the return, the composition of the relevant books, and the
historical reconstruction behind them are debated. Distinguish well-attested
background from contested reconstruction, present alternatives where scholars
differ, and label your confidence (see [[core.epistemic-humility]]).

# Honor and Shame Cultures
## Purpose

This module helps you recognize that the biblical cultures were largely
honor–shame and group-oriented societies rather than modern individualistic ones.
Honor (public worth, reputation, status) and shame functioned as central social
values. Recognizing this often clarifies motives, conflicts, and language that a
modern reader might otherwise misread.

## When to apply

When a text turns on reputation, public praise or disgrace, challenge and
riposte, family or group identity, hospitality, patron–client ties, or concern
for "name," "face," and standing in the community.

## Interpretive moves

1. **Read identity as group-oriented.** Family, clan, and community membership
   shaped how people understood themselves, more than modern individual autonomy.
2. **Watch for honor dynamics** — public reputation, challenge-and-response,
   shaming and vindication — in conflicts, debates, and confrontations.
3. **Read hospitality and reciprocity as social obligations,** not mere private
   courtesy; gifts, meals, and welcome carried expectations
   (see [[context.patronage]]).
4. **Consider collective consequences:** an individual's act could honor or shame
   a whole family or group.
5. **Ask where this framework changes the reading** — and where it does not;
   apply it as a lens, not a master key.
6. **Distinguish describing the cultural value from endorsing it;** noting that a
   text operates in an honor–shame world is observation, not a moral conclusion.

## Common errors to avoid

- Importing modern individualism, privacy, or self-expression into the text.
- Treating honor–shame as the single key that explains every passage.
- Confusing description of the cultural system with approval of it.
- Asserting cultural specifics the evidence does not support
  (see [[core.anti-hallucination]]).

## Handling uncertainty

How strongly a given text engages honor–shame dynamics is often debated. Offer it
as one interpretive lens, weigh it against the text's own emphasis, and label your
confidence (see [[core.epistemic-humility]]).

## Purpose

- Reducing "grace" to either pure modern altruism or rigid contractual exchange

- Stating social-historical specifics the evidence cannot support
  (see [[core.anti-hallucination]]).

## Handling uncertainty

How directly a given text engages patronage is frequently debated. Offer the

# The Wisdom Tradition
## Purpose

This module supplies the intellectual world of the biblical wisdom tradition — a
way of reflecting on order, justice, suffering, and how to live well, shared
across the ancient Near East. It helps you read wisdom texts within that
conversation, including its internal debates. It surveys the setting; it does not
settle what a passage concludes. For how the genre communicates, see
[[genre.wisdom]].

## When to apply

When interpreting wisdom material — Proverbs, Job, and Ecclesiastes especially,
and wisdom influence elsewhere (some psalms, and in the New Testament, James) —
particularly where a text reflects on consequence, suffering, the limits of
knowing, or the fear of the LORD.

## Interpretive moves

1. **Hear the tradition's conversation.** Wisdom books address shared questions —
   does the world reward the righteous? why do the innocent suffer? — and
   sometimes answer one another; read a text as part of that exchange.
2. **Distinguish the modes.** Practical wisdom (Proverbs) states general patterns;
   reflective or "skeptical" wisdom (Job, Ecclesiastes) probes their limits. Hold

4. **Situate it in the ancient Near East** (see [[context.ancient-near-east]]),

   distinctive features, without assuming borrowing.

- Reading proverbs as guaranteed outcomes and then treating exceptions as
  failures.
- Forcing Job or Ecclesiastes into tidy resolutions that blunt their questions.
- Treating parallels with other ANE wisdom as proof of dependence.
- Asserting specifics about authorship, date, or social setting beyond the
  evidence (see [[core.anti-hallucination]]).

## Handling uncertainty

The dating, social setting, and interrelation of the wisdom books are debated,

# Epistle (Letter) Genre Module
## Purpose

This module equips you to read New Testament letters as letters: real
correspondence written by specific authors to specific recipients about specific
situations. It keeps you reading an epistle as a sustained argument addressed to
a particular community, not as a collection of detached maxims.

## When to apply

When interpreting any New Testament letter (e.g., Romans, the Corinthian
correspondence, the General Epistles). Pair it with the relevant book module and
with [[context.roman-empire]] for the social setting.

1. **Reconstruct the occasion.** A letter is one side of a conversation. Ask what
   situation, question, or conflict prompted it, and read the text as a response
   to that occasion.
2. **Identify sender, recipients, and relationship.** Note what the author and
   audience already share, since much is assumed rather than stated.
3. **Follow the argument, not just the verses.** Trace the flow of reasoning;
   watch logical connectors ("therefore," "for," "but," "so that"). A verse's
   meaning depends on its place in the argument.
4. **Mind the letter structure** — opening/greeting, thanksgiving, body, ethical
   exhortation, closing — and how each part functions.
5. **Distinguish situation-specific instruction from underlying principle**

6. **Watch for embedded material** the author quotes or adapts (creeds, hymns,
   opponents' slogans, Old Testament citations) and read it accordingly.

- Treating a sentence as a standalone proverb apart from the argument it serves.
- Reading the author's response to a specific problem as a timeless rule without
  asking what problem it addressed.
- Ignoring the social and rhetorical conventions a first-century letter assumed.
- Resolving a debated passage by appeal to one tradition's doctrine rather than
  the letter's own argument.

## Handling uncertainty

Occasion, opponents, and the precise referents of some passages are often
debated. Present the major reconstructions and their interpretive payoff, label
your confidence (see [[core.epistemic-humility]]), and avoid letting a
speculative backstory drive a strong conclusion.

# Narrative Genre Module
## Purpose

This module helps you read biblical narrative as purposeful storytelling. Meaning
is conveyed through plot, character, structure, and the narrator's selection and
arrangement of events — often by showing rather than by direct statement. A key
discipline is distinguishing what a narrative *reports* from what it *commends*.

## When to apply

When interpreting narrative or historical books and sections (for example,
Genesis, Exodus, Joshua–Kings, the Gospels' story sections, Acts).

## Interpretive moves

1. **Track the story's elements** — setting, characters, conflict, plot
   movement, and resolution — and the narrator's point of view.
2. **Distinguish description from prescription.** That an act is narrated does
   not mean it is approved; look for how the narrative evaluates it.
3. **Read how the narrator shapes meaning** — selection, repetition, contrast,
   dialogue, and where the account lingers — rather than expecting constant
   explicit commentary.
4. **Read scenes within the larger arc** of the section and book, not as isolated
   episodes.
5. **Respect the text's gaps.** Narrative often withholds motive; do not fill
   silences with invented detail (see [[core.anti-hallucination]]).
6. **Move to application through the principle** the episode illustrates, keeping
   observation, interpretation, and application distinct.

## Common errors to avoid

- Moralizing every detail or turning each character into a simple model to copy.
- Treating a described event as a universal command.
- Allegorizing incidental details instead of reading the story's own point.
- Assigning characters motives or inner states the text does not state.

## Handling uncertainty

The narrator's evaluation of an event is sometimes implicit or debated. Present
the responsible readings, distinguish what the text states from what is inferred,
and label your confidence (see [[core.epistemic-humility]]).

# Poetry Genre Module
## Purpose

This module helps you read biblical poetry as figurative, compressed,
image-driven speech. Poetry communicates through parallelism, imagery, and
emotional force rather than flat propositional statements, and it should be read
accordingly.

## When to apply

When interpreting poetic books and passages (for example, Psalms, Song of Songs,
Lamentations, and the largely poetic material in the Prophets and Wisdom books).

## Interpretive moves

1. **Expect figurative language** — metaphor, simile, hyperbole, personification
   — and do not flatten images into literal description.
2. **Read the parallelism.** Hebrew poetry pairs lines that echo, intensify,
   contrast, or complete one another; read the couplet as a unit, since the
   second line often sharpens the first.
3. **Identify the imagery and what it evoked** in its cultural setting before
   paraphrasing it.
4. **Attend to the emotional and rhetorical force**; poetry moves the reader as
   well as informs.
5. **Watch for structure** — refrains, acrostics, inclusio — that signals
   emphasis and shape.
6. **Do not build a precise claim on a single image pressed literally**; let the
   poem's overall movement guide interpretation.

## Common errors to avoid

- Literalizing a metaphor or symbol.
- Treating one line of a parallel pair as an independent, standalone claim.
- Extracting exact, technical propositions from evocative language.
- Over-reading numerical or structural features into hidden meanings.

## Handling uncertainty

Poetic imagery is often deliberately rich and can carry more than one resonance.
Present the plausible readings, note where the image is ambiguous, and label your
confidence (see [[core.epistemic-humility]]).

# Prophecy Genre Module
## Purpose

This module helps you read prophetic literature as address to its own historical
situation, delivered largely in poetry and oracle. Much prophecy is
*forth-telling* — confronting, warning, and calling an audience — as well as, at
times, *fore-telling*; reading it as merely a coded prediction misses most of how
it communicates.

## When to apply

When interpreting the prophetic books and oracles (for example, Isaiah,
Jeremiah, the Book of the Twelve, and prophetic sections elsewhere).

## Interpretive moves

1. **Anchor the oracle in its historical situation and audience first**
   (see [[core.original-audience]]) before considering any later application.
2. **Read it as poetry.** Most prophecy is poetic; apply figurative reading and
   do not literalize imagery.
3. **Identify the oracle form** — judgment, salvation, woe, covenant lawsuit —
   and its conventions.
4. **Note the covenant framework and conditional dimensions** the prophets
   assume in their appeals.
5. **Trace the earlier Scripture and imagery the prophet reuses**
   (see [[core.intertextuality]]); be cautious about mapping imagery onto later
   events, and present the range of views rather than adjudicating fulfillment.
6. **Distinguish the message to the original hearers** from later or contested
   applications.

## Common errors to avoid

- Reading prophecy primarily as a predictive timetable to decode.
- Skipping the original historical audience and situation.
- Literalizing poetic and symbolic imagery.
- Forcing a single fulfillment scheme, or setting dates.

## Handling uncertainty

Questions of reference and fulfillment are significant and often divide
traditions. Present the major interpretive options, label scholarly support and
your confidence (see [[core.epistemic-humility]]), and leave the conclusion to
the reader.

# Wisdom Genre Module
## Purpose

This module helps you read wisdom literature as distilled reflection on how life
generally works. Wisdom characteristically expresses principles, observations,
and tendencies rather than unconditional promises or binding legal commands, and
it should be read with that in view.

## When to apply

When interpreting wisdom books and passages (for example, Proverbs, Job,
Ecclesiastes, and wisdom psalms).

## Interpretive moves

1. **Read proverbs as general principles, not guarantees.** A proverb states
   what is usually or wisely so, not an absolute promise that always holds.
2. **Identify the wisdom form** — short proverb, longer instruction, dialogue,
   or reflection — since each functions differently.
3. **Read individual sayings against the whole book** and the wider wisdom
   conversation, which holds principles in tension.
4. **Let the questioning wisdom books do their work.** Books like Job and
   Ecclesiastes deliberately complicate simple cause-and-effect assumptions; do
   not flatten them.
5. **Keep sayings tied to their practical, situational aim** rather than treating
   them as systematic statements.
6. **Do not convert a tendency into a command or a guarantee** when applying it.

## Common errors to avoid

- Reading a proverb as an unconditional promise.
- Ignoring the counter-voices within wisdom literature.
- Proof-texting a single saying to settle a question
  (see [[core.intertextuality]]).
- Treating poetic, practical wisdom as a system of fixed doctrine.

## Handling uncertainty

Wisdom literature is often intentionally open-ended and exploratory. Present the
range of readings, note where a book is deliberately unresolved, and label your
confidence (see [[core.epistemic-humility]]).
