# Glossary

Neutral, working definitions of terms used across BHF modules. These describe
*method and scholarship*, not doctrine.

- **Hermeneutics** — the theory and method of interpretation; the principles by
  which a text is understood.
- **Exegesis** — drawing meaning *out of* a text by attending to its language and
  context (the goal).
- **Eisegesis** — reading meaning *into* a text from outside it (the error to
  avoid).
- **Genre** — the kind of literature a text is (narrative, law, poetry, wisdom,
  prophecy, apocalyptic, gospel, epistle, etc.); genre sets the rules for how a
  text communicates.
- **Intertextuality** — the literary relationship between texts, where one text
  quotes, alludes to, echoes, or develops another; in BHF, an observable feature
  of how biblical authors use earlier Scripture, not a doctrinal claim.
- **Allusion** — an indirect reference to another text through shared wording or
  imagery, weaker than a direct quotation and stronger than a faint echo.
- **Pericope** — a self-contained unit of text (a scene, a paragraph, a saying)
  treated as a whole.
- **Original audience** — the people to whom a text was first addressed, whose
  situation and assumptions shape its meaning.
- **Observation / Interpretation / Application** — the three stages of careful
  reading: what the text says; what it meant to its first audience; what it might
  mean for readers today.
- **Literary context** — the surrounding text (paragraph, section, book, canon)
  that constrains a passage's meaning.
- **Diatribe** — a rhetorical style using posed questions and answers, common in
  ancient argumentation (e.g., parts of Romans).
- **Paraenesis** — moral exhortation; practical instruction on how to live.
- **Semantic range** — the set of meanings a word can carry; context selects which
  applies in a given occurrence.
- **Verbal aspect** — the way a verb portrays an action (e.g., as ongoing or as a
  whole), distinct from the time of the action.
- **Patron / client (patronage)** — the Greco-Roman system of reciprocal
  obligation between a benefactor and a dependent.
- **Koine Greek** — the common Greek of the Hellenistic and Roman periods in which
  the New Testament was written.
- **Confidence labels** — BHF's shared vocabulary for signaling certainty:
  *consensus*, *majority view*, *minority view*, *speculation* (see
  `core.epistemic-humility`).
- **Proof-texting** — citing an isolated verse to settle a question without regard
  to its context; an error BHF guards against.
