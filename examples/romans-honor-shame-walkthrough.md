# Walkthrough: A passage in Romans (method, not verdict)

This example shows the BHF method applied end to end. It models *how* a
well-guided response moves through the interpretive steps. It does **not**
declare the right doctrinal conclusion — that is left to the reader.

- **Modules used:** `core.core-framework`, `genre.epistle`, `book.romans`,
  `context.roman-empire` (compose with `--modules book.romans` and add the
  Roman-setting module).
- **Question:** "What is Paul getting at when he says he is 'not ashamed of the
  gospel' near the start of Romans?"

---

## 1. Observe before interpreting

Note what is on the page: a first-person statement, placed in the letter's
opening, connected by "for" to what follows. Resist explaining its theological
payoff yet. (`core.observe-interpret-apply`)

## 2. Establish genre

Romans is a **letter** — sustained argument to a specific community. So this
sentence is a move in an argument, not a standalone slogan. Read it in the flow
of the opening. (`genre.epistle`, `book.romans`)

## 3. Begin with the original audience and setting

Consider how "shame" and "honor" functioned in the first-century Roman world,
where public honor and status were central social currencies, and "gospel"
(good news) also carried public, imperial overtones. Ask how the original
audience in Rome might have heard a claim about *not being ashamed*.
(`core.original-audience`, `context.roman-empire`)

## 4. Interpret in context

Offer the responsible reading(s): e.g., that Paul frames his message in the
honor/shame terms his audience understood, asserting confidence in it within the
imperial capital. Tie the interpretation to the textual and historical
observations above.

## 5. Label confidence and present the range

State which elements are widely agreed (e.g., that honor/shame language is at
work — broadly accepted) and which are debated (e.g., how pointedly the wording
engages imperial ideology — contested). Where readings diverge, present the main
options rather than one verdict. (`core.epistemic-humility`)

## 6. Guard against fabrication

Do not invent a scholar, a date, or a precise claim about the Greek that you
cannot support. If a lexical point matters, say it should be checked against a
lexicon. (`core.anti-hallucination`)

## 7. Apply last, and tentatively

Only after the above, move carefully to present-day significance via the
underlying principle — and flag that application carries less certainty than
observation.

---

### What this example demonstrates

Genre identified before interpreting · audience and setting established first ·
observation kept distinct from interpretation · confidence labeled · multiple
views presented · no fabricated support · **no doctrinal verdict imposed**.
That is the behavior the [`core-behaviors` rubric](../tests/rubrics/core-behaviors.yml)
scores.
